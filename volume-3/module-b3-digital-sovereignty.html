Digital sovereignty and DPI models
Four regions built the digital future differently. What can each learn from the others — and what does that mean for the next 50 years?

There is no single model for how a society builds digital public infrastructure. Some countries have made identity and payments a public utility, open and interoperable. Others have let private platforms fill the same role at massive scale. Others are still building, navigating debt, vendor lock-in, and political pressure simultaneously.

Each approach encodes a set of choices about who controls data, who can access services, who profits, and who is trusted. Those choices compound over decades. A payment architecture built in 2010 shapes what's possible in 2040.

"Digital infrastructure is never neutral. Every design decision is also a political decision about power, access, and accountability."

This track asks you to become a close reader of at least two models — ideally one near your own region and one that challenges your assumptions. You will compare how they handle a specific dimension of trust: identity, payments, data exchange, or governance. And you will write a scenario that draws a lesson from that comparison and applies it somewhere it hasn't been applied yet.

Who this track is for

This track works best for Connectors and Critics — people who see across systems and are energised by finding the insight that one region's experience offers to another. It also suits policy analysts, development economists, and anyone embedded in a government digital transformation programme.

You don't need deep technical knowledge. You need rigour, curiosity, and a willingness to be genuinely changed by what you find.

Activity — 45 to 60 minutes
Go deeper on one model you don't know well

Choose the model furthest from your own experience. Spend 45 minutes reading — primary sources where possible (government strategy documents, official reports, academic papers) rather than just news coverage.

  • What problem was this model designed to solve? What was the founding moment or crisis?
  • Who governs it, and who is accountable when something goes wrong?
  • What does "trust" mean in this model — trust between whom, enforced how?
  • What would a citizen with no technical knowledge need to believe for this model to serve them well?

Write a half-page summary. This becomes a source document for your scenario.

The comparison activity
The lesson lives in the gap between two models — not inside either one.

Comparison is not about ranking systems from best to worst. It is about finding the question that one model's experience answers for another — and asking whether that answer travels.

Choose two models to compare. Pick one you know well and one you researched in the previous module. Focus your comparison on a single dimension — don't try to compare everything at once.

Choose a comparison dimension

Pick one of these five — the one most relevant to your region or your scenario question:

  • Identity: who issues it, who controls it, who can revoke it
  • Payments: who runs the rails, who profits, who is excluded
  • Data governance: where data lives, who can access it, what rights citizens have
  • Resilience: what happens under attack, disaster, or political pressure
  • Inclusion: who is served, who is left out, and whether the gap is closing

  • What has Model A learned — the hard way — that Model B hasn't had to learn yet?

Writing your scenario
A scenario is not a prediction. It is a rigorous, structured argument about what could happen — and what it would require.

The best scenarios in this track will not be abstract. They will be set in a specific place, at a specific moment — a government deciding which DPI model to adopt, a regional body negotiating an interoperability standard, a community discovering that the system they rely on has been designed for someone else.

Your scenario should be 1,500 to 3,000 words. It will be submitted to the IEEE DH50 repository and may be presented at the Santa Clara celebration in November 2026 if selected. Write it as if a policymaker in your target region will read it — and act on it.

Scenario structure — five sections
1
The setting (200–300 words)
Name the place, the moment, and the decision. What is at stake, for whom, and by when? Ground the reader immediately in something specific and real.
2
What the models show (400–600 words)
Present the comparison you built in the previous module. What has been learned, elsewhere, that is directly relevant to this setting? Cite your sources. Be specific about what worked, what failed, and under what conditions.
3
The lesson that travels (300–400 words)
This is the core of your scenario. What is the transferable insight — the thing that another region can actually use? Be honest about what does not travel: every model is shaped by its context, and false universalism is worse than no lesson at all.
4
What would need to be true (300–500 words)
Scenarios are conditional. What political, technical, or social conditions would need to hold for the lesson to apply? What are the most likely failure modes? Who would resist this, and why would they be right to?
5
The question you're leaving open (200–300 words)
A good scenario does not close every door. End with the question your research could not answer — the thing someone in that region, with better access, would need to investigate next. This is not a weakness. It is intellectual honesty, and it is an invitation to the next ambassador.
Activity — ongoing, 2 to 4 weeks
Interview at least one practitioner

Before you finalise your scenario, talk to someone who has built or governed DPI in your region or in one of the models you're studying. Your comparison table will have raised questions that no document can answer.

  • What decision, in retrospect, shaped everything else?
  • What did you get wrong that you wish you'd known earlier?
  • What is the question you think the outside world consistently fails to ask about your system?

You don't need their answers to change your scenario. You need them to make it honest.

Reflection prompt
After your first full draft
  • What did you have to cut that you wish you could have kept — and what does that tell you about scope?
  • Which of your sources would disagree with your conclusion — and have you engaged with that disagreement honestly?
  • Who is the most important person who should read this, and have you written it for them?
