Module A — Foundations · A1
A
From keys to trust: the foundation
You don't need to understand the math. You need to understand what the math makes possible.

Digital trust is not abstract. It is the reason you can buy something from a stranger on the other side of the world and have a reasonable expectation they will send it. It is the reason a hospital in Tallinn can share a patient record with a clinic in Helsinki without anyone intercepting it. It is the reason a billion people in India can receive government payments directly, without a bank in the middle taking a cut.

All of this runs on a small set of mathematical ideas, most of them traceable back to the Diffie-Hellman-Merkle paper of 1976. Understanding those ideas — not the equations, but the logic — is the foundation of your work as a Future Ambassador.

Three ideas to hold onto
1
Public-key cryptography
Two mathematically linked keys: one shared openly, one kept private. What one locks, only the other can open. This makes it possible to communicate securely with strangers.
2
Digital signatures
A way to prove a document hasn't been tampered with, and that it came from who it claims to come from — without revealing your private key. The foundation of trust in digital contracts, identity, and records.
3
Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI)
The open, interoperable systems — identity, payments, data exchange — that cryptographic trust makes possible at national scale. India's Aadhaar and UPI, Estonia's X-Road, and Singapore's Singpass are all examples.

These three ideas stack on each other. Public-key cryptography makes digital signatures possible. Digital signatures make DPI trustworthy. Trustworthy DPI makes it possible for governments and economies to function digitally at scale.

As a Future Ambassador, your work will almost certainly touch one of the layers in this stack — even if it focuses on policy, inclusion, or governance rather than technology. Knowing where your question lives in the stack helps you see who you need to talk to and what's already been built beneath you.

Activity — 20 minutes
Trace the trust chain in one transaction

Pick any digital transaction you completed today — a payment, a login, a signed document, a health or government service. Then work backwards:

  • Where is a digital signature involved? (Even if you didn't see one.)
  • Who issued the certificate or credential being used?
  • What would break if that certificate were compromised or no longer trusted?
  • Who — if anyone — has no access to this transaction because they lack the required infrastructure?

You don't need to find perfect answers. The goal is to develop the habit of seeing trust infrastructure where it's invisible.

Reflection prompt — log entry
After tracing the trust chain
  • Which layer surprised you most — and why?
  • Where was the trust chain weakest or most opaque?
  • Did this change which scenario track you want to pursue?
Module A — Foundations · A2
The quantum threat — and what comes next
The math beneath the internet was built for a world without quantum computers. That world is ending.

Every time you send an encrypted message, your device and the recipient's device agree on a shared secret using mathematics that depends on a simple fact: certain problems are extraordinarily hard to reverse. Multiplying two large prime numbers together is easy. Factoring the result back into those primes is, with today's computers, practically impossible.

A sufficiently powerful quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could factor those numbers in hours. Most estimates suggest that kind of quantum computer is somewhere between 10 and 20 years away — but no one is certain, and the infrastructure we're building now will still be running when it arrives.

"Harvest now, decrypt later." Some adversaries are already collecting encrypted data today, betting they will be able to read it in the future. The threat isn't waiting for quantum computers to exist.

The response is called post-quantum cryptography (PQC): a new generation of algorithms designed to be hard for quantum computers to crack. In 2024, NIST finalised its first set of post-quantum standards. The question is no longer whether to transition — it is how, how fast, and who gets left behind in the process.

The transition challenge in three parts
T
Technical: replacing 50 years of infrastructure
Cryptographic algorithms are embedded in hardware, firmware, and software across billions of devices. Many can be updated remotely. Some — industrial controllers, medical implants, legacy government systems — cannot.
P
Political: who sets the standards?
NIST's PQC standards reflect US priorities. Other countries — notably China, which has its own PQC programme — may not adopt them. A fragmented global transition creates new interoperability gaps and trust boundaries.
E
Equity: the transition burden is not evenly shared
High-income countries with modern infrastructure can upgrade relatively quickly. Countries running older systems, or dependent on foreign vendors, face a harder path — and a longer window of vulnerability.
Activity — 30 to 45 minutes
Map the transition risk in your region

Research one of the following questions for your country or region and write a one-page summary of what you find:

  • Has your government published a post-quantum migration strategy? If so, what does it prioritise? If not, what does that silence suggest?
  • Which critical systems in your region — financial, health, identity, energy — are most exposed to "harvest now, decrypt later" attacks?
  • Are there legacy systems (infrastructure more than 10 years old, hardware that cannot be updated remotely) that will be difficult or impossible to migrate?

Your one-page summary is the seed of a Module B scenario. Keep it — you'll return to it.

Reflection prompt — log entry
After researching the transition risk
  • What was hardest to find out — and what does that gap tell you?
  • Who in your region is responsible for this transition, and do they know it?
  • What would need to be true for your region to complete a post-quantum transition successfully by 2035?

Module A — Foundations · A3
Map your region's digital infrastructure
The most important activity in Module A. Draw what exists, name what's missing, and locate yourself in the picture.

Every scenario you investigate in Module B will be rooted in a specific place. Before you can imagine what the next 50 years might look like in your region, you need a clear picture of what exists today — not the official narrative, but the ground truth.

This activity asks you to build a simple map — not a technical architecture diagram, but a strategic one. What DPI layers exist in your country or region? Who built them, who runs them, who is excluded from them? What's fragile, what's contested, and what's genuinely missing?

You will almost certainly not be able to fill in every cell. The gaps are part of the map.
