Welcome to the mission
This is not a manual. It is a field guide.

In 1976, two mathematicians at Stanford published a paper that changed how every human being on earth communicates. You almost certainly used their idea this morning, to check your messages, pay for something, log into an account. You probably didn't notice. That's the point.

Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman, working with Ralph Merkle, solved a problem that had stumped cryptographers for centuries: how do two strangers share a secret over an open channel, with no courier, no prior contact, no shared codebook? Their answer became the foundation of the secure internet.

Fifty years later, we're marking that anniversary. But this is not a commemoration. It is an expedition.

"The next 50 years of the internet will be shaped by the decisions we make now — and by who gets to make them."

As a Future Ambassador, your job is to imagine those decisions clearly, rigorously, and publicly. You will pick a corner of the future that fascinates you, post-quantum security, AI governance, digital sovereignty, inclusion, and you will research it, think hard about it, and share what you find with the world.

This field guide will walk you through that journey. You don't need to read it front to back. Pick it up anywhere. Follow your curiosity. Come back when you need a framework, a template, or a prompt to get unstuck.

Module 0.1 — Orientation
The 50-year story in 5 minutes
A hook, not a lecture.

Imagine you want to send a locked box to a stranger. The problem is that any lock requires a key, and if you send the key, anyone could intercept it and open the box. For centuries, this was the fundamental problem of secure communication. You could only communicate privately with someone you'd already met in person, already shared a secret with.

In 1976, Diffie and Hellman proposed something that seemed almost paradoxical: what if you could lock the box with information you shared openly, in public, and only the recipient could unlock it? What if the locking and unlocking used different keys, mathematically related but practically impossible to reverse-engineer?

"It was as if someone had figured out how to build a padlock that anyone could close, but only the owner could open."

This became public-key cryptography. It made the commercial internet possible. Every time you see a padlock icon in your browser, every time a digital signature confirms a document is authentic, every time a government system verifies your identity, that's the DHM idea, running silently underneath.

Now the foundations are shifting. Quantum computers threaten to crack the mathematics that DHM depends on. AI is generating content that can no longer be trusted at face value. Billions of people are being brought onto digital infrastructure that may or may not be trustworthy. The next 50 years will require the same kind of creative leap that Diffie, Hellman, and Merkle made in 1976.

That's where you come in.

Activity — 15 minutes
Explain it without math

Choose one of the following ways to explain public-key cryptography to someone who has never heard of it. Don't use equations. Don't use the word "algorithm."

  • Use a physical object as a metaphor (a lock, a paint colour, a safety deposit box)
  • Tell it as a short story with two characters who need to share a secret
  • Draw it, a diagram, a comic strip, a map

Then actually explain it to someone, a friend, a family member, a colleague. Note what they ask. Their questions are data.

Reflection prompt
After explaining it
  • What part was hardest to explain, and why?
  • What question did they ask that you couldn't answer?
  • What metaphor landed best?

Write your answers in your ambassador log.

Module 0.2 — Orientation
What kind of Future Ambassador are you?
This points you toward the modules that will suit you best.

Future Ambassadors come from everywhere, graduate students, policy analysts, engineers, journalists, community organisers. The scenario you choose to investigate should match not just your interests, but how you work and what you bring.

Read the four profiles below. Pick the one that fits closest, or the two that split your attention. Then check which B-track modules they point toward.

The builder
You ask: "What should we build?"
You're drawn to technical possibility — systems, infrastructure, protocols. You think in architectures.
The critic
You ask: "What's missing or broken?"
You notice gaps, failures, and inequities. You're energised by what hasn't been fixed yet.
The connector
You ask: "Who needs to talk to whom?"
You see relationships between domains — policy, technology, community. You translate across worlds.
The narrator
You ask: "How do we tell this story?"
You make complex ideas accessible. You write, speak, film. Your superpower is public clarity.
Reflection prompt
Before you choose your scenario track
  • Which region or country are you most embedded in, and what's the most urgent digital infrastructure question there right now?
  • What would a meaningful contribution from you look like by November 2026?
  • Who in your network could you collaborate with, or learn from?
