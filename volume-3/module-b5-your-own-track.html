Module B — Scenario Tracks · Track B5
Your own track
The question that wouldn't fit anywhere else.

Tracks B1 through B4 were written by people who don't know you, your region, your community, or the specific corner of the next fifty years that you find yourself thinking about at odd hours. They cover a lot. They don't cover everything.

B5 exists because the most important contributions to this anniversary will probably not be the ones that fit neatly into a predetermined category. They will be the ones that noticed something the category system missed — a question specific to a region, a sector, a technology, a community, or a moment in time that none of the other tracks anticipated.

"The question that won't leave you alone is usually the one worth asking."

This track gives you the scaffold to pursue that question rigorously — to test whether it's genuinely distinct from the existing tracks, to build your own analytical structure, and to connect your work to the broader network of ambassadors in a way that makes its contribution legible.

It does not give you the content. That part is yours.

Before you commit to B5

B5 is not an easier path than B1–B4. It is a harder one — because you are responsible for the analytical structure as well as the content, and because a scenario that is merely different from the other tracks is not the same as one that is better for being different.

Ask yourself: does my question require a new track, or does it fit within an existing one approached from a different angle? A question about child safety online might be a B2 scenario focused on a specific governance gap. A question about post-quantum transition in a specific region might be a B1 scenario with a tighter geographic scope. If an existing track can hold your question, use it.

If, after that test, your question still doesn't fit — you're in the right place.

B5 and the handbook's future
B5 scenarios that prove generative — that open a territory other ambassadors want to explore — become the seed of future tracks. If your question resonates widely enough, the next version of this handbook may include a B6 that wouldn't exist without you. Document your analytical structure carefully, even as you build it. It is the contribution that outlasts the scenario itself.
Module B · Track B5
Test your question
Four tests. If your question passes them all, it's worth a track of its own.

A good B5 scenario begins with a question that is specific, consequential, tractable, and genuinely connected to cryptographic trust infrastructure — even if that connection is oblique. These four tests are designed to help you assess whether your question meets that bar before you build the rest of your work around it.

Is it specific enough to be answerable?
Not "what is the future of digital identity?" but "what happens to informal cross-border labour when verifiable digital credentials become mandatory for work authorisation in the Gulf states?"
Is it consequential enough to matter?
If the answer to your question is "not much changes either way," it's probably a footnote to someone else's scenario, not a track of its own. What hangs on the answer?
Is it tractable in the time you have?
Can you make meaningful progress on this question between now and October 2026? A question that requires a decade of fieldwork is a research agenda, not a scenario. Scope it until it's achievable.
Does it connect to the DHM legacy?
Not every question needs cryptography at its centre — but there should be a visible thread. Where does digital trust infrastructure appear in your question, even in the background?
Activity — 30 minutes
Write your question three ways

Take the question driving you toward B5 and write it three times — each time sharper and more specific than the last.

  • Version 1: the broad version, as it first occurred to you
  • Version 2: narrowed to a specific region, sector, or moment in time
  • Version 3: narrow enough that you could outline an answer in 2,000 words

Version 3 is your working question. If you cannot get from Version 1 to Version 3, the question may not yet be ready for a scenario — or it may need a collaborator who can help you scope it.

Reflection prompt
The honest check
  • Which of the four tests is your question weakest on — and what would it take to strengthen it?
  • Who else, anywhere in the world, is working on this question — and have you found them yet?
  • If you had to explain to a B3 or B4 ambassador why your question deserves its own track, what would you say?
Module B · Track B5
Build your scaffold
The structure is yours to design. These blocks are the connective tissue that keeps it part of the handbook.

Each block below corresponds to something every B-track scenario must contain — regardless of which track it belongs to. They are the minimum connective tissue that makes your work legible to other ambassadors, to the global repository, and to the next person who arrives at a similar question.

Open each block, read what it requires, and write your own version of it — specific to your question, your evidence, and your analytical approach.

1
What you're investigating — and why it belongs here
Your question, your track name, your case for why it's distinct
▶
Every B5 scenario begins with a declaration: here is my question, here is why it doesn't fit an existing track, and here is what it will contribute that wouldn't exist otherwise. This is not throat-clearing — it is the analytical foundation of your track.
  • What is your working question — Version 3 from the previous tab?
  • Which existing track comes closest to holding it — and what does it leave out?
  • What is the specific contribution your scenario makes that the existing tracks don't?
2
Your primary evidence — what you're building on
What exists, what you found, who you talked to
▶
Every track has its primary evidence type: B1 has the cryptographic inventory; B3 has the comparative model analysis; B4 has the listening conversations. B5 requires you to name yours — what is the evidence your scenario is built on, and why is it the right evidence for your question?
  • What is your primary evidence type — technical audit, policy analysis, fieldwork, comparative research, something else?
  • What existing work are you building on — and what gap in that literature is your scenario filling?
  • Who have you talked to, and what did they tell you that written sources couldn't?
3
Your analytical structure — the five sections
Named for your question, not borrowed from another track
▶
Every B-track scenario has five sections. The sections in B1, B2, B3, and B4 are named for what those specific questions require. Yours should be too. Name your five sections — not as generic headings, but as specific descriptions of what each section will do for your particular argument.
  • What does your opening section need to establish — and what is the right evidence to open with?
  • What is the analytical core of your scenario — the section where the real argument lives?
  • How does your scenario end — with a decision, an open question, a prediction, a community's knowledge, or something else?
4
Your connection to the network
How your track connects to the other four — and to the ambassadors working them
▶
A B5 track that is entirely isolated from the other four is a missed opportunity. Even the most novel question will have contact points with post-quantum transition, AI governance, digital sovereignty, or inclusion — because these are not separate problems. They are facets of the same set of decisions about the next fifty years of digital infrastructure.
  • Where does your question intersect with B1, B2, B3, or B4 — even tangentially?
  • Which ambassador working another track would most benefit from reading your scenario?
  • What would a future track building on your work look like — and who might write it?
5
Your reflection questions
Write the prompts your track asks — for yourself and for the ambassadors who come after
▶
Each track has reflection prompts — the questions an ambassador asks themselves after completing the core activity and after their first draft. Write yours. They should be specific to the analytical and ethical challenges of your question. They will become part of the handbook if your track is adopted for future versions.
  • What is the hardest question your scenario forces you to answer honestly?
  • What assumption, if wrong, would invalidate your central argument?
  • What would you tell the next ambassador who arrives at a similar question with different evidence?
Submit your scaffold, not just your scenario
When you submit your B5 scenario to the IEEE DH50 repository, include your completed scaffold as an appendix — with the five section names, the evidence type, and the connection map. This is what makes your track replicable. The scenario is your contribution to this anniversary. The scaffold is your contribution to the next one.

Log and completion
What you built, what it cost, and what it opens up.

B5 asks more of you than any other track — not in time or expertise, but in self-direction. You had to decide what counted as evidence, what structure served your argument, and what questions your track needed to ask.

That work is not just the content of your scenario. It is the methodological contribution of your track. The log entry below asks you to make it explicit.

Log entry — Track B5 complete
Submitting your scenario and scaffold

Submit your completed scenario and your scaffold appendix to the IEEE DH50 repository via your regional coordinator or the ambassador portal. In your submission notes, flag it as a B5 track and include your proposed track name. Scenarios received by October 2026 are eligible for presentation at the Santa Clara Final Celebration.

The handbook's editors will review B5 submissions after the anniversary to identify tracks that generated interest from multiple ambassadors. If yours is among them, you may be invited to co-author a new track for the next edition.
