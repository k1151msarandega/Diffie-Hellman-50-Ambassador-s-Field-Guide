Section D — Ambassador Log and Global Record
The year in full
Everything you wrote in passing, gathered into something that lasts.

When you opened this handbook — whenever that was, wherever you were — you wrote a first log entry. You named the question that drew you here, the person you would tell, the future you were trying to understand. That entry is probably still honest. But it is probably also incomplete in ways you could not have anticipated at the time.

Section D is where the year becomes visible as a whole. The log entries you made after each module are not a collection of separate notes — they are a record of how your thinking developed, where it surprised you, where it stalled, and what it eventually produced. That record has value beyond the scenario paper. It is a document of how a serious person engaged seriously with a hard problem over the course of a year.

"The log is not a record of what you did. It is a record of who you became while doing it."

Section D has four parts: a complete log index that lets you review everything you wrote in one place; the Global Connector exchange, which is the last active task in the handbook; a global ambassador record entry that contributes your work to the shared network; and a final log entry — the last thing you write before closing the handbook.

What Section D produces

Three things leave this section and enter the world:

  • Your global ambassador record entry — a structured contribution to the DH50 network that other ambassadors and coordinators can find and build on
  • Your Global Connector exchange — a documented conversation with an ambassador in another region that earns the final badge and feeds the network's cross-fertilisation
  • Your final log entry — a private document, for you alone, that marks the end of the year and the beginning of whatever comes next
Section D · Module D1
Your full log
One place to see the whole year — and to notice what you didn't see at the time.

Every module in this handbook ended with a log entry. Some were short — a single sentence. Some were extended — a full reflection after a difficult interview or a salon that didn't go as planned. Together they form a record that is worth reading in one sitting before you write anything else in Section D.

Use the index below to confirm which entries you've completed — then read through them in order. Don't edit. Don't improve. Just read. You are looking for the moments where something shifted, where a question changed shape, where you wrote something that turned out to be wrong.

Read your log in one sitting

Once you've confirmed which entries you've completed, read through all of them in order — from the first entry in Module 0.3 to the most recent. Read as a stranger would: someone who has never met you, reading the record of a year's serious work.

  • What is the arc — how did your thinking move from the first entry to the last?
  • Where was the sharpest turn — the moment where something you believed was wrong, or something you hadn't considered became central?
  • Is the person who wrote the first entry recognisably the same person who wrote the last one?

Write three sentences answering those questions. They become the opening of your final log entry.

Section D · Module D2
The Global Connector exchange
Knowledge multiplied is not the same as knowledge shared. The exchange has to be real.

Every track in Section B ended with a log prompt asking you to name an ambassador in a different region whose work connects to yours. The Global Connector badge is earned by following through on that prompt — by actually having the exchange, documenting what each of you brought to it, and recording what neither of you could have arrived at alone.

The exchange is not a courtesy call. It is the moment where the handbook's network logic becomes real — where the knowledge that was generated in isolation meets the knowledge generated elsewhere, and something new becomes visible in the gap between them.

1
Identify your connector
Return to the ambassador you named at the end of your B-track log. Contact them through your regional coordinator or the DH50 ambassador portal. If that person is not reachable, ask your coordinator to match you with an ambassador whose scenario track intersects with yours from a different regional perspective.
2
Prepare one thing to bring
Before the exchange, identify one specific finding from your scenario — not a summary of your whole paper, but one thing that surprised you, that you think the other ambassador's context would either confirm or challenge. Come with that one thing ready to offer. The exchange works when both people arrive with something specific, not when one person interviews the other.
3
Have the exchange — 60 minutes
The format is a conversation, not a presentation. Each person shares their one specific finding and asks the other: does this hold in your context? Where does it diverge? The divergences are the data. Spend more time on the places where your findings don't travel than on the places where they do.
4
Document the multiplied insight
After the exchange, each ambassador writes a short note — no more than 200 words — describing the insight that emerged from the conversation that neither person could have arrived at alone. This note is attached to both scenario submissions in the global repository.

Why this badge is last

The Global Connector badge is the only one in the handbook that requires another person. That is not an accident. The scenario you wrote is a contribution to the global record. The exchange is a contribution to the global intelligence — the emergent understanding that arises when findings from different contexts are put in conversation.

The NIC-of-NICs framework that underpins this initiative describes knowledge as non-rivalrous: when you share it, you keep it, and the act of sharing creates something new. The exchange is where you experience that logic directly.

Section D · Module D3
The final entry
The last thing you write in this handbook. Take your time with it.

Every previous log entry in this handbook was prompted by a module — something you had just done, just found, just heard. This one is different. There is no module before it. It follows from the year as a whole, from reading your log in one sitting, from the exchange with your connector ambassador, from whatever has happened in the months between your first entry and this one.

The global ambassador record that follows is structured and public — it will be visible to other ambassadors and to coordinators. This final entry is yours alone. It will not be submitted anywhere. Write it for the person who will open this handbook in a few years, wonder who used it, and find your name in the record — and for the version of yourself who returns to it and needs to remember why this year mattered.
