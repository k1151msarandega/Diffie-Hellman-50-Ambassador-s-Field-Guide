Digital inclusion and the trust gap
Connectivity without trust isn't enough. And trust without voice isn't trust at all.

The story of the internet's first fifty years has often been told as a story of expansion — more people online, more services digitised, more of daily life mediated by connected systems. The gaps that remain are framed as a connectivity problem: bring people online, and they gain access to everything the digital world offers.

But the communities that have been excluded longest know something the expansion narrative tends to overlook. Getting online is not the same as being served. Being served is not the same as being trusted. And being trusted is not the same as having any say in how the systems that govern your life are designed.

"The opposite of exclusion is not access. It is agency — the ability to participate in the decisions that shape the infrastructure you depend on."

Cryptographic trust infrastructure — digital identity, verifiable credentials, secure payments, data rights — can extend genuine agency to people who have never had it. Or it can replicate the exclusions of every previous system, at greater scale and with more permanence. Which of those futures is arriving depends on choices being made right now, mostly by people who are not from the communities most affected.

This track is for ambassadors who are close to those communities — by origin, by work, by long relationship — and who want to bring that proximity to bear on a question that too often gets answered from a distance.

Who this track is for — and a note on posture

This track is built for community organisers, development practitioners, educators, social researchers, and anyone whose work keeps them in close contact with people who are underserved by digital infrastructure. It is also for policymakers and technologists who want to do that work with more honesty about what they don't know.

Useful posture
Witness and translator
You are close to a community. You listen carefully. You translate what you hear into terms that people with power can act on — without losing what made it true.
Posture to watch for
Expert from outside
You analyse a community's exclusion from the outside, propose solutions that weren't asked for, and present findings without checking them against the people they describe.

Neither posture is about where you were born or what your credentials are. It is about how you work. This track will prompt you to check your posture at each stage.

A note on this track's open-source nature
More than any other track, B4 will be enriched by ambassadors who bring contexts, languages, and communities that no single author could anticipate. The activities and prompts here are starting points. If they don't fit your community — if the questions are wrong, if a framework doesn't translate, if an assumption is revealing its origins — change them. Document what you changed and why. That documentation is a contribution to the handbook itself.
Before you start — 15 minutes
Your starting position

Write one honest sentence for each in your log:

  • Which community are you closest to — and what is the nature of that closeness?
  • What digital infrastructure does that community depend on most — and where does it fail them?
  • What would you need to find out before you could write honestly about that failure?

That last question is the most important. Come back to it as you work through the track.

The gap beneath the gap
Connectivity is the visible gap. Trust infrastructure is the one underneath it — and it compounds every other exclusion.

When digital inclusion is discussed in policy circles, it is usually framed as a connectivity problem: broadband coverage, device access, digital literacy. These are real gaps and they matter. But there is a second gap that receives far less attention, and that sits underneath the first one in ways that make it harder to see and harder to fix.

It is the gap in trustworthy digital infrastructure — identity systems that work for people without formal documents; payment rails that serve people without bank accounts; data rights that apply to people whose data is collected but whose consent is never sought; credential systems that recognise qualifications earned outside Western academic institutions.

This gap is not a side effect of exclusion. In many cases, it is its mechanism. The same populations that lack connectivity also lack the identity documents that digital identity systems require. The same communities that struggle to access formal financial services are the ones whose informal savings and lending systems are invisible to digital payment infrastructure. Fixing connectivity without fixing trust infrastructure does not close the gap — it changes its shape.

Four dimensions of the trust gap
I
Identity
An estimated one billion people worldwide lack any form of legal identity. Digital identity systems built on official documents exclude them by design. Systems that don't require prior documentation — like India's Aadhaar — make different trade-offs that carry their own risks for vulnerable populations.
P
Payments
Mobile money has brought financial access to hundreds of millions — but informal economies, cross-border remittances, and community savings systems often remain outside formal rails, subject to fees, exclusion, or surveillance that formal participants don't face.
D
Data rights
The communities whose data is most commercially valuable — for training AI, for public health surveillance, for agricultural planning — are often the communities with the least legal protection over how that data is used, shared, or sold.
V
Voice in design
Infrastructure built without the participation of the communities it serves tends to serve them badly — not through malice, but through the systematic absence of their knowledge. Who sits in the rooms where DPI standards are written? Whose failure modes are anticipated in the design?
Activity — 45 to 60 minutes
Map the trust gap in your community

Work through the four dimensions above for your community. For each one, write a paragraph — not a list — that describes the gap as someone inside the community would describe it. Not "access to formal identity is limited" but what it actually means to navigate a world of digital services without recognised identity: which doors are closed, what workarounds exist, what the workarounds cost.

  • Which dimension is most acute — and is that the one most visible to outsiders?
  • Where does the community have its own trust infrastructure — informal, oral, relational — that digital systems don't recognise?
  • What does the community trust that the formal system doesn't — and what does the formal system trust that the community can't access?
Reflection prompt — posture check
After mapping the trust gap
  • Did you write about the community, or did you write for it — and do you know the difference in this case?
  • Which part of the map is drawn from your own experience, which from direct conversation with community members, and which from secondary sources? Be explicit about the difference.
  • What would someone from inside the community correct, add, or push back on in what you've written?
Section B · Track B4
Stories from the edge
Not case studies. Stories — because the detail is where the truth lives.

The other scenario tracks are built around frameworks: models to compare, algorithms to assess, governance mechanisms to evaluate. This one is built around stories, because the exclusion that B4 is trying to document is not primarily visible in aggregate data. It is visible in the specific, irreducible detail of what happens when a system meets a person it was not designed to serve.

The examples below are starting points — documented cases from different contexts where trust infrastructure failed, excluded, or actively harmed the communities it was supposed to serve. Read them not as cautionary tales but as listening exercises: what does this tell you about what the designers did not know, or did not ask?

Aadhaar and the biometric exclusion problem — India
India's Aadhaar system has enrolled over 1.3 billion people — a genuinely remarkable achievement of digital inclusion at scale. But for tens of millions of people, mostly manual labourers, elderly persons, and those with skin conditions or disabilities, the system's biometric authentication fails repeatedly. A fingerprint worn smooth by years of agricultural work cannot reliably authenticate. A person who cannot authenticate cannot access their ration entitlement. The system's inclusion was real — and its exclusion mechanism was built into its authentication design.
The failure was not anticipated because the people most at risk of it were not consulted in the design process. They were the intended beneficiaries — not the intended participants in design. The distinction matters enormously for how you think about inclusion.
Listening question: In your community, what physical or social characteristic — a kind of work, a life circumstance, a form of identity — would a biometric or document-based system fail to recognise? Who would be asked to prove they exist to a system that cannot see them?
Remittances and the cost of trust — Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia
Global remittances to low- and middle-income countries exceed $650 billion annually — more than foreign direct investment and overseas development aid combined. For many recipient communities, this money is the primary source of income. And yet the average cost of sending $200 internationally is around 6%, with corridors to Sub-Saharan Africa averaging over 8%. The fees are not a technical necessity — they are the cost of using trust infrastructure (correspondent banking, AML compliance, identity verification) that was built for a different class of transaction.
Mobile money and crypto-adjacent payment systems have reduced costs on some corridors. They have also introduced new risks: exchange rate volatility, platform failure, regulatory uncertainty, and the loss of the human relationships that informal hawala networks provided. Inclusion in formal payment systems is not always an improvement over exclusion from them, when the formal systems are designed for someone else.
Listening question: In your community, what informal trust systems — tontines, hawala, community savings groups, word-of-mouth credit — exist because formal systems can't or won't serve these needs? What do those systems do that digital infrastructure doesn't recognise or replicate?
Land rights and digital registries — East Africa and Southeast Asia
Digital land registries have been proposed and implemented in several countries as a way to reduce corruption, secure land tenure, and enable smallholder farmers to use land as collateral. The logic is sound. The implementation has often been devastating. In several cases, the process of digitising land records has overwritten customary and communal land rights — forms of tenure that were real, recognised within communities, and governed by oral tradition and social consensus — because they had no paper documentation to scan.
The digital system did not deny that customary rights existed. It simply could not see them. And what the system cannot see, it cannot protect. The communities most dependent on customary tenure — women, smallholders, indigenous groups — found that the digitisation of land records that was supposed to secure their rights had instead made those rights invisible at exactly the moment when visibility mattered most.
Listening question: In your community, what rights, relationships, or forms of property exist that are real and recognised within the community but have no formal documentation? What happens to them when a digital system arrives that can only see what is written down?
AI, gig platforms, and the invisibility of informal work — Global South
Gig economy platforms — ride-hailing, delivery, domestic work matching, freelance labour — have extended economic participation to workers who were previously excluded from formal employment. They have also created a new class of exclusion: workers who are deeply embedded in a platform's infrastructure, whose income depends on algorithmic allocation, but who have no employment relationship, no data rights, no recourse when the algorithm fails, and no voice in how the system that governs their working lives is designed.
For workers in the Global South, these dynamics are intensified by currency risk, regulatory gaps, and the fact that many platforms are owned and governed in jurisdictions whose legal systems those workers cannot access. The AI systems that allocate their work, assess their performance, and can deactivate their accounts were trained largely on data from other workers, in other contexts, with different characteristics. The failure modes that emerge from that mismatch are felt locally, but the accountability sits elsewhere.
Listening question: In your community, who works within or is governed by digital platform infrastructure that they had no part in designing? What do they know about how it works — and what are they not permitted to know?
Core activity — 2 to 4 weeks
Listen before you map

Before you complete the gap mapping table in the next module, conduct at least three conversations with people inside your community who experience the trust gap you're studying. These are not interviews in the extractive sense — you are not collecting data. You are listening for what the frameworks above do not capture.

  • What is the most recent moment you were excluded from something because a system couldn't recognise you or your situation?
  • What workaround do you use — and what does that workaround cost you in time, money, dignity, or risk?
  • If you could change one thing about how this system works, what would it be — and who would you need to talk to, to change it?
  • Is there anything about your situation that you think I'm likely to misunderstand?

That last question is the most important. Ask it every time. Write down the answers verbatim wherever possible. With permission, these voices can appear directly in your scenario — not as data points, but as testimony.

Section B · Track B4
Writing your scenario
Start with a person. End with a structural change. Hold the distance between them honestly.

B4 scenarios are built differently from the other tracks — not because the analytical standard is lower, but because the evidence is different. Your primary sources are conversations and lived experience, not NIST standards or NIST comparative data. Your argument is carried as much by narrative as by analysis. And your reader needs to be reached in a different way: not convinced by technical authority, but moved to recognise something they should have already known.

This does not mean B4 scenarios are soft. The structural analysis — what exactly is broken, who designed it that way, what it would take to change — must be rigorous. It just begins differently.

Scenario structure — five sections
1
One person's experience (300–400 words)
Begin with a specific person — with their permission, either named or anonymised as they prefer — and a specific moment when a trust infrastructure failure affected their life. Not a composite, not a hypothetical. A real encounter between a real person and a system that could not see them. Write it as you heard it. Do not analyse it yet. Let it be what it is.
2
What the system cannot see (300–500 words)
Now step back. What is the structural condition that made that experience possible? Which of the four dimensions of the trust gap — identity, payments, data rights, voice in design — is the operative one here? What did the designers of this system fail to anticipate, and why? Draw on your gap mapping and your listening. Be specific about the design choices that created the failure, not just the failure itself.
3
What exists elsewhere that could help (300–400 words)
Reference at least one example — from another country, another sector, another community — where a similar gap has been addressed more successfully, or where an approach is being tried that is worth attention. Be honest about whether it travels: what would need to be different for it to work in your context? What is the risk of importing a solution designed for someone else's problem?
4
What would need to change — and who would need to change it (300–500 words)
This is where the narrative meets the structural. What specific change — in design, in policy, in governance, in procurement, in standard-setting — would have prevented the experience you opened with? Who has the power to make that change? Who currently benefits from things staying as they are? And crucially: what role, if any, does the affected community have in making that change happen — or is the scenario proposing that change be done to them, again?
5
What the community already knows (200–300 words)
End not with a recommendation to the powerful, but with an acknowledgement of the knowledge that already exists in the community — the workarounds, the informal systems, the hard-won expertise in navigating infrastructure that was not built for them. The best DPI will build on that knowledge, not replace it. Your scenario should end by making that knowledge visible, and by naming what would be lost if the proposed solution ignored it.
Before you submit — a final check
Three questions that only B4 asks
  • Have the people whose experience grounds your scenario read it — and have you incorporated what they said in response?
  • Does your proposed change increase the community's agency in shaping the infrastructure it depends on, or does it merely improve the quality of what is done to them?
  • If this scenario were read aloud in the community it describes, would the people there recognise themselves in it — and would they feel respected?

If the answer to any of these is no, or uncertain, go back before you submit. The standard for this track is not lower than the others. It is different.

Log and completion
What you heard, what you understood, and what you still got wrong.

Every scenario in this track is incomplete. The communities most excluded from digital infrastructure are also the communities most excluded from the rooms where research is discussed, publications are read, and policy is made. Your scenario will reach some of those rooms — it will not reach the community it describes, unless you carry it there yourself.

The log entry below includes a prompt that no other track has: what you got wrong, and how you know. Include it. It is not a weakness in your submission — it is what makes it trustworthy.
