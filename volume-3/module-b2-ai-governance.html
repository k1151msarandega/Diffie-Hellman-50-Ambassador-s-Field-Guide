AI governance and verifiable trust
AI can generate anything. Cryptography can prove what's real. The question is whether we build that bridge — and who controls it.

For fifty years, cryptographic signatures have answered a deceptively simple question: did this come from who it claims to come from, and has it been changed since? A signed document, a verified identity, a timestamped record — these are statements about provenance and integrity that a third party can check without trusting the sender.

Generative AI has made that question newly urgent. When any piece of content — text, image, audio, video, code, scientific data — can be synthesised at negligible cost and indistinguishable quality, the infrastructure of trust that underpins journalism, law, medicine, elections, and commerce begins to strain. The tools exist, in principle, to sign AI-generated content, to watermark model outputs, to verify the provenance of training data. The question is whether we will deploy them, at what cost, and who will govern them.

"The problem is not that AI can deceive. The problem is that we have not yet decided what infrastructure we want to build so that it doesn't have to."

This track sits at the intersection of cryptography, AI policy, and institutional design. It is not primarily a technical track — though understanding what's technically possible matters. It is a track about governance: who decides what gets verified, by what means, under what rules, with what accountability.

Who this track is for

This track is built for Connectors and Narrators who are drawn to the policy and institutional dimensions of AI — but it has meaningful technical depth for Builders willing to engage with governance questions. It suits AI policy researchers, legal and regulatory analysts, journalists and media professionals, and anyone working on digital identity or content authenticity.

It is also the right track if your primary question is about a specific sector — electoral integrity, scientific publishing, legal evidence, healthcare AI — rather than AI governance in the abstract. The most useful scenarios in this track are tightly scoped: one domain, one decision, one place.

You do not need to be a cryptographer. You need to understand enough about what cryptographic tools can and cannot do to assess whether a proposed governance approach is technically coherent — and to call out the ones that aren't.

The core tensions
Four fault lines that run through every AI governance and trust debate. Your scenario will sit on at least one of them.

The governance debates around AI and verifiable trust are not primarily technical disagreements. They are disagreements about values — about who should have power over what can be verified, what can be attributed, and what can be trusted. Understanding the fault lines before you choose your scenario focus will save you from building a technically elegant proposal that is politically impossible — or a politically popular one that doesn't work.

Select a tension below to explore its logic and what it means for scenario design.

Tension 1
Verification vs. anonymity
Proving provenance requires attribution. Attribution threatens anonymity. Both values are real.
Tension 2
Openness vs. control
Open standards enable interoperability. Proprietary systems enable accountability. Hard to have both.
Tension 3
Voluntary vs. mandated trust
Voluntary adoption is slow. Mandated adoption creates gatekeepers. Every governance model picks a side.
Tension 4
Global standards vs. digital sovereignty
Common standards reduce fragmentation. National systems protect against foreign control. Both matter.

Section B · Track B2
Cases in the wild
Real attempts to build cryptographic trust into AI systems — what they tried, what they got right, and what they didn't.

The governance of AI outputs is not a purely theoretical problem. Several serious attempts are already underway — from content provenance standards to watermarking initiatives to regulatory mandates. None of them is fully mature. All of them are instructive. Your scenario will be stronger if it is built on top of what has already been tried, rather than proposing something that has already been attempted and found wanting.

C2PA — Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity
C2PA is an open technical standard for attaching cryptographically signed provenance metadata to digital content — images, video, audio, documents. A C2PA manifest records what tool created the content, what edits were made, and by whom, creating a verifiable chain of custody. It is backed by Adobe, Microsoft, Google, the BBC, and others, and is being embedded into camera hardware, editing software, and publishing platforms.
The standard works as designed: content signed under C2PA can be verified against the signer's certificate. The harder problem is adoption and incentives. Signing requires participating in the ecosystem; unsigned content carries no penalty. A reader cannot distinguish between content that was unsigned because it was created before C2PA existed, content whose creator chose not to participate, and content that was deliberately generated without provenance metadata to avoid detection.
The scenario question: In your domain, what would need to be true — technically, legally, or institutionally — for C2PA adoption to become the default rather than the exception? Who would need to mandate it, and under what authority?
AI watermarking — invisible attribution for model outputs
AI watermarking embeds imperceptible signals into model-generated content — statistical patterns in text, pixel-level modifications in images — that can be detected by the model's operator. Unlike C2PA, watermarks do not require the receiver to trust a certificate chain; detection is performed by the model provider. The EU AI Act mandates watermarking for certain AI-generated content; the White House voluntary AI commitments (2023) included watermarking as a commitment from major developers.
The technical limitations are significant. Text watermarks are fragile — paraphrasing, translation, or even minor editing can remove them. Image watermarks are more robust but can be stripped by post-processing. Watermarks are also fundamentally operator-controlled: only the model provider can detect them, which raises accountability questions if the provider is also a party to a dispute. And open-source models cannot be watermarked in any durable sense — anyone can remove the watermarking layer.
The scenario question: For your domain, is watermarking a meaningful governance tool — or a compliance theatre that shifts responsibility from institutions to technology? What would a watermarking regime that actually changed behaviour look like?
eIDAS 2.0 — Digital identity as the anchor for AI trust
The EU's revised eIDAS regulation (2024) requires member states to offer citizens a European Digital Identity Wallet — a verifiable credential system enabling authenticated, privacy-preserving claims about identity and qualifications. The wallet uses selective disclosure: a user can prove they are over 18 without revealing their date of birth, or prove they hold a professional qualification without revealing their employer.
The connection to AI governance is indirect but significant: eIDAS 2.0 provides an identity infrastructure that could anchor provenance in AI-assisted professional contexts — legal submissions, medical records, financial advice, educational assessments — where the human professional's verified identity needs to be attached to AI-augmented outputs. It also creates a model for how verifiable credentials can preserve privacy while enabling accountability, which is the core design challenge for most AI governance proposals.
The scenario question: In your domain, what would it mean to attach a professional's verified digital identity to an AI-assisted output? Who bears responsibility when the output is wrong — the professional, the AI provider, or the institution? Does eIDAS 2.0 provide the architecture to answer that question, or does it only reframe it?
EU AI Act — Regulatory mandates for transparency and traceability
The EU AI Act (in force 2024, phased implementation through 2027) establishes risk-tiered obligations for AI systems deployed in the EU. High-risk systems — including those used in healthcare, employment, education, law enforcement, and critical infrastructure — must meet requirements for transparency, human oversight, and technical documentation. Providers must maintain logs enabling post-hoc traceability of system outputs.
The Act's traceability requirements create a de facto demand for something like cryptographic audit trails in high-risk AI deployments — without mandating any specific technical approach. This creates a gap that technical standards bodies, vendors, and national regulators are actively trying to fill. It also creates jurisdiction questions: an AI system developed in the US, deployed via an API, used by a professional in Germany to generate a medical report — who is responsible, under which regulation, and what technical evidence would be needed to establish that?
The scenario question: For your domain, what would a technically coherent implementation of the EU AI Act's traceability requirements actually look like? Is the cryptographic infrastructure to support it available, deployed, or still missing — and what is the cost of the gap?

Section B · Track B2
Writing your scenario
One domain. One governance gap. One concrete proposal — and an honest account of why it might fail.

The AI governance space is full of high-altitude proposals — principles, frameworks, voluntary commitments — that are never tested against the messy reality of a specific domain. Your scenario should be the opposite: ground-level, specific, and grounded in a real incident or documented gap. It should be legible to a policymaker, a technologist, and a domain practitioner simultaneously — which means it cannot be purely technical or purely political. It must hold both.

Your scenario should be 1,500 to 3,000 words, and it should be written for a reader who has authority in your domain — a regulator, a professional body, a platform operator, a standards organisation — and who is currently doing nothing about this problem, probably because they don't yet know what "doing something" would actually mean.

Scenario structure — five sections
1
The incident (200–300 words)
Open with the real case you found. What happened? Who was harmed? What would a reader in your domain recognise as the core failure? Do not begin with statistics or policy context — begin with the event. Make your reader feel why this matters before you explain what to do about it.
2
The governance gap (300–400 words)
What made the incident possible? Map the gap precisely: not "there is no regulation" but "there is no mechanism for X to verify Y before Z occurs." Be specific about what cryptographic or institutional infrastructure is missing — and what exists elsewhere (C2PA, eIDAS, watermarking, audit trails) that could address part of the gap if deployed differently.
3
The proposal (400–600 words)
Describe a governance mechanism that would close or significantly narrow the gap. Be technically coherent: if your proposal relies on cryptographic signatures, explain whose key infrastructure, governed how, revocable by whom. If it relies on watermarking, acknowledge the open-source problem. Name the tension your proposal sits on — verification vs. anonymity, mandatory vs. voluntary — and explain the trade-off you are making and why.
4
The political analysis (300–400 words)
Use the stakeholder map you built in the previous module. Who benefits from your proposal? Who loses? Who would resist it, and do they have the power to block it? What is the minimum viable coalition needed to move this forward — and does it currently exist? This is not a section to soften or skip. A proposal without political analysis is a wish, not a scenario.
5
The next incident (200–300 words)
Unlike B1 and B3, which end with a decision or an open question, B2 ends with a prediction: if your proposal is not adopted, what is the next incident that will occur in your domain — and roughly when? Make it specific. This is not alarmism; it is what rigorous scenario planning does. It makes the cost of inaction visible in the same concrete terms as the opening incident.
Activity — 1 to 2 weeks
Interview someone who has been inside a governance failure

The most valuable interviews for this track are not with AI researchers or policymakers — they are with people who have experienced, at close range, a breakdown of trust in AI outputs in your domain. A journalist who published a fabricated quote. A lawyer whose opposing counsel submitted an AI-hallucinated citation. A radiologist whose AI-assisted diagnosis was wrong in a way no human would have been. A moderator whose platform failed to detect synthetic media in an election campaign.

  • What did you know, and when did you know it — and how did you find out the content wasn't what it claimed to be?
  • What would you have needed to have in place to catch this before it caused harm?
  • What stopped you from having that — was it technical, institutional, financial, or political?
  • What do you think your sector is still not taking seriously enough?
Reflection prompt
After your first full draft
  • Is your proposal technically coherent — have you checked it against someone who understands the cryptographic infrastructure it depends on?
  • Have you been honest about the tension your proposal sits on, or have you written around it?
  • Is the "next incident" prediction specific enough to be falsifiable — or is it vague enough to be unfalsifiable and therefore useless?
