Post-quantum transition
The mathematics beneath the internet will break. The question is not whether — it is whether we will be ready.

Every secure connection on the internet — every HTTPS handshake, every digital signature, every encrypted file — is protected by mathematics that depends on problems a classical computer cannot solve in any reasonable amount of time. Factoring large numbers. Computing discrete logarithms. The security of the internet is, at its foundation, a bet on computational hardness.

Quantum computers do not play by the same rules. Shor's algorithm, running on a sufficiently large and stable quantum computer, would reduce those hard problems to tractable ones. The padlock does not get harder to pick — it gets a different kind of lock altogether, one the old key opens trivially.

"We are in a race between the maturation of quantum hardware and the global migration of cryptographic infrastructure. The hardware side of that race does not wait for the migration side to catch up."

In 2024, NIST published its first finalised post-quantum cryptography standards — the result of an eight-year global competition. The starting gun for transition has officially been fired. What follows is the largest coordinated infrastructure migration in the history of computing, and it is happening largely out of public view.

Your job in this track is to make one specific part of that transition visible, legible, and actionable — for a region, a sector, or a class of system that you understand better than most. The hardest problems in this transition are not algorithmic. They are logistical, political, and deeply human.

Who this track is for

This track is built for Builders and Critics with technical grounding — engineers, security researchers, systems architects, and technically-minded policy analysts. You don't need to be a cryptographer; you need to understand systems well enough to audit them and imagine their failure modes.

It is also a track for anyone working in critical infrastructure — healthcare, finance, energy, government — who has wondered whether their organisation is prepared. The answer, in most cases, is not yet. This track will help you understand what "prepared" actually means.

Narrators are welcome here too — this is one of the most consequential and least-understood transitions of our era, and people who can explain it clearly to non-technical audiences are urgently needed.

Urgency note
Unlike most technology transitions, post-quantum migration cannot wait until quantum computers arrive. Data encrypted today under classical algorithms can be harvested now and decrypted later, once quantum hardware matures. For long-lived secrets — classified documents, health records, financial archives, national security communications — the window for safe transition may already be closing.

The new algorithms
What NIST standardised, why it matters, and what it doesn't solve.

The post-quantum algorithms standardised by NIST in 2024 are not drop-in replacements for RSA or elliptic curve cryptography. They are based on different mathematical problems, have different performance profiles, and make different trade-offs between security, speed, and key size. Understanding those trade-offs is the starting point for any serious transition plan.

The number of engineers who understand both classical and post-quantum cryptography is small. Most organisations do not yet know which of their systems are cryptographically vulnerable. The audit has not happened.

As you read, note:

  • What problem is each algorithm designed to solve — and what is it explicitly not designed to solve?
  • What does NIST say about transition timelines and the deprecation of classical algorithms?
  • What is not addressed in the standards that your target system or sector will need to figure out for itself?

The cryptographic inventory
You cannot migrate what you cannot see. Most organisations have never fully mapped their cryptographic dependencies.

A cryptographic inventory is a systematic audit of every place an organisation uses cryptography: which algorithms, for what purpose, in which systems, with what key lifetimes. It sounds straightforward. In practice, for any organisation older than ten years, it is a significant undertaking — cryptographic dependencies are embedded in libraries, firmware, third-party services, and legacy systems whose original developers may no longer be reachable.

The inventory activity below is a structured exercise — not a full enterprise audit, but a rigorous enough mapping to identify the highest-priority vulnerabilities in a system or organisation you know well. It is also the research backbone of your scenario paper.

Scope this carefully
Choose a single system, organisation, or sector you have genuine access to — through your work, your research, or a practitioner relationship. A shallow inventory of a national healthcare system is less useful than a thorough inventory of a single hospital's records infrastructure. Go deep rather than wide.

Activity — 45 to 60 minutes
Find what your organisation doesn't know it has

After completing the inventory above, investigate at least one dependency you did not initially think to include:

  • Third-party libraries and open-source dependencies (run a software composition analysis if you have access)
  • Hardware security modules, smart cards, or embedded devices that implement cryptography in firmware
  • External services your system trusts — certificate authorities, timestamp services, authentication providers
  • Backup and archive systems, which may hold data encrypted years ago under algorithms no longer considered secure

Add what you find to the inventory. The surprises are the most valuable part of this exercise.

  • Which high-risk item surprised you most?

Writing your scenario
A transition plan no one has written yet — for a system or sector you understand better than most.

The post-quantum transition is not one problem. It is thousands of overlapping, partially interdependent migration projects happening simultaneously across every sector of the global economy. What's missing is not a global strategy — NIST, ENISA, NCSC, and others have produced plenty of those. What's missing is ground-level visibility into specific systems, specific sectors, and specific regions where the transition is most urgent and least resourced.

Your scenario should fill one of those gaps. It should be 1,500 to 3,000 words, grounded in your cryptographic inventory, and written for a reader who has authority over the system or sector you're describing — a CISO, a health ministry official, a central bank technologist, an infrastructure regulator.

Scenario structure — five sections
1
The system at risk (200–300 words)
Name the system or sector. Describe its cryptographic profile in non-technical terms: what does it protect, for how long, and for whom? Establish why quantum risk is not a distant abstraction for this system — it is a present-tense data governance problem.
2
What the inventory revealed (400–600 words)
Present the findings from your cryptographic inventory. Where are the high-harvest-risk assets? Which dependencies are updatable, and which are not? Be specific about what you found and what you couldn't find — both are informative. If you consulted practitioners, cite what they told you.
3
The migration path (300–500 words)
Propose a sequenced approach. What should be migrated first, and why? What are the dependencies — the things that cannot move until something else has moved first? Where does a hybrid approach (running classical and post-quantum algorithms in parallel during the transition) make sense, and where does it create its own risks? Be concrete: name the algorithms, the systems, the order of operations.
4
The blockers (300–400 words)
What will prevent or slow this transition? Be honest about blockers that are not technical: budget cycles, procurement rules, vendor lock-in, skills gaps, organisational inertia, regulatory uncertainty. The most useful scenarios name the real obstacles — not the ones that are comfortable to mention.
5
The decision that needs to be made (200–300 words)
End with one concrete, bounded decision that your reader has the authority to make — and that would meaningfully advance the transition if they made it. Not a vague recommendation to "prioritise post-quantum readiness." A specific action: commission an audit, amend a procurement standard, fund a pilot migration, convene a working group with a defined deliverable. Make it easy to say yes.
Activity — 1 to 2 weeks
Interview a practitioner in your target sector

Before finalising your scenario, speak with someone who manages or secures infrastructure in your target system or sector. Your inventory will have surfaced questions that only an insider can answer.

  • Has your organisation begun a post-quantum readiness assessment? If not, why not?
  • What is the single biggest obstacle to starting a migration in your organisation?
  • What would you need to see — evidence, mandate, funding, or something else — to prioritise this in the next budget cycle?
  • What do you wish the security research community understood better about the operational realities of systems like yours?

That last question often produces the most useful material. Write the answer down verbatim if you can.

Reflection prompt
After your first full draft
  • Is the decision you're asking your reader to make genuinely within their power — or have you asked for something that requires someone above them to act first?
  • Have you named the blockers honestly, including the ones that are politically uncomfortable?
  • Could a technically literate non-specialist read this and understand what's at stake? If not, where does it lose them?
