Foundation
Module A.0 — Before 1976

For most of human history, secure communication required something that sounds trivially obvious: if you wanted to send a private message, you and the recipient had to share a secret first. A codebook, a key, a prior meeting in a private place. Kings used couriers. Armies used physical codebooks distributed in person before battle. Banks used dedicated secure lines. The military had elaborate key distribution networks.

This was not merely inconvenient. It was a hard structural limit on who could communicate privately. You could only have a confidential exchange with someone you had already met — or someone connected to you through a chain of trusted intermediaries. Strangers could not share secrets over open channels. That was simply not possible.

"The problem was not that ciphers were weak. The problem was more fundamental: any cipher strong enough to be useful required sharing a key — and sharing a key over an open channel defeated the purpose."

Think about what this meant in practice. If a bank in London wanted to communicate securely with a correspondent bank in Singapore, someone had to physically carry a key between them first. If a company wanted to open a secure communication channel with a new supplier, they had to establish trust through a chain of physical contact before a single encrypted message could be sent.

The entire structure of secure communication was built on the assumption that private exchange required prior physical contact. For five thousand years, from Egyptian scribes to Second World War cipher machines, this assumption held. Nobody seriously believed it could be otherwise.

1
The key distribution problem
To encrypt a message, sender and receiver need to agree on the same key. But if you send the key over an open channel, anyone intercepting it can read all future messages. So the key has to travel securely — which requires another secure channel — which requires another key. The problem recurses infinitely.
2
Why this mattered for scale
For small, closed systems — a military unit, a diplomatic corps — physical key distribution was expensive but workable. For open, global communication between strangers, it was impossible. The commercial internet, as we know it, could not have been built on symmetric cryptography alone.
3
What a solution would have to do
It would have to allow two parties who had never met — who shared no prior secret — to establish a shared secret over a channel that anyone could listen to. To most cryptographers in 1975, this sounded like a contradiction in terms.
Why this matters for your community engagement
The weight of the problem is what makes the solution astonishing. Before you explain what Diffie and Hellman did, make sure your audience feels what had seemed impossible. A solution that arrives without a sense of the problem it solved will feel like a neat trick rather than a genuine breakthrough. The "impossible problem" is your opening — the thing that makes the room lean in.
Activity — 15 minutes

  • Think of a moment when someone in your community needed to trust a stranger digitally — a payment, a login, a document — without any prior physical contact. What made that possible? What would have happened if it weren't possible?

Module A.1 — New Directions in Cryptography, 1976
A note on the anniversary
The DH50 celebration marks the publication of New Directions in Cryptography by Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman in the IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, November 1976. That paper — eleven pages, modestly titled — is what this anniversary commemorates. Ralph Merkle's contribution to the ideas that preceded it was real and significant, and we acknowledge it in the genealogy. But the 1976 IEEE paper is Diffie and Hellman's. When you represent this anniversary, that distinction matters.

In November 1976, Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman published a paper that dissolved the key distribution problem entirely — not by making key transport more secure, but by making it unnecessary.

Their insight, which Diffie later described as arriving in a single evening of thought, was this: what if a key didn't have to be the same thing for locking and unlocking? What if you could separate those two functions — so that one key, shared openly with the world, could lock a message, while only a different, private key could unlock it?

This was the concept of public-key cryptography. And it rested on something that seems almost paradoxical: two parties who have never met can establish a shared secret over a completely open channel — with an eavesdropper listening to every word — and the eavesdropper still cannot reconstruct the secret.

The mechanism that makes this possible — the Diffie-Hellman key exchange — relies on a beautiful mathematical asymmetry. Certain operations are easy to perform in one direction and practically impossible to reverse. Multiplying two large prime numbers together is fast. Factoring the result back into those primes is, for large enough numbers, computationally infeasible. This asymmetry is the lock — and it requires no physical key to be transported anywhere.

Here is exactly how it works. Work through the four steps, then try the interactive demonstration below.

1
Public setup — agreed by everyone, in the open
Alice and Bob agree on two public numbers that anyone can know: a large prime number p and a base generator g. In the demo below, p = 23 and g = 5. In real systems, p has hundreds of digits. An eavesdropper can see both numbers — that's fine.
2
Private keys — chosen secretly, never shared
Alice secretly picks a private number a. Bob secretly picks a private number b. Neither tells the other. These numbers never leave their hands.
3
Public exchange — sent openly, revealing nothing
Alice computes A = ga mod p and sends A to Bob — openly, over the public channel. Bob computes B = gb mod p and sends B to Alice. An eavesdropper sees both A and B. That's fine — knowing A and B is not enough to reconstruct the private numbers.
4
Shared secret — arrived at independently, by both
Alice takes Bob's public value and computes s = Ba mod p. Bob takes Alice's public value and computes s = Ab mod p. Because of the commutative property of modular arithmetic, both calculations produce the same number — the shared secret. Neither sent it. The eavesdropper cannot derive it from A and B alone.

Module A.2 — The first branch: Digital Signatures

Diffie and Hellman's 1976 paper did something that the best scientific papers do: it solved one problem so cleanly that it immediately revealed the next one. They had shown how two strangers could establish a shared secret. But the paper went further — it sketched a possibility that even Diffie and Hellman hadn't fully solved yet: digital signatures.

A shared secret lets two parties communicate privately. But what about a different problem: how do you prove that a message came from who it claims to come from — and hasn't been altered since? In the physical world, we use handwritten signatures and notaries. These work because a signature is hard to forge and easy to verify by someone who knows your handwriting. Could mathematics do the same thing?

Within a year of the 1976 paper, three mathematicians at MIT — Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Len Adleman — used the public-key framework Diffie and Hellman had opened to build exactly that. RSA, published in 1977, gave the world its first practical digital signature scheme. The logic inverts the key exchange: instead of using a public key to lock a message that only a private key can unlock, a digital signature uses a private key to sign a message that anyone with the corresponding public key can verify.

"The key exchange said: we can share a secret without meeting. The digital signature said: I can prove this came from me — mathematically, verifiably, without a notary's office anywhere in sight."

This was the first branch growing from the trunk. And it was generative in its own right: digital signatures eventually became the foundation of digital certificates, certificate authorities, code signing, legally binding electronic documents, land registries, and the entire infrastructure of institutional trust online.

It cannot be bribed, and it does not require trust in the verifier's judgement — only trust in the mathematics.

Module A.3 — The genealogy begins

You have now seen the trunk of the tree and its first branch. But look at what those two ideas — key exchange and digital signatures — made possible together. They gave the world a way to communicate privately with strangers, and a way to prove the origin and integrity of any digital object. That combination is the foundation on which nearly everything else in the digital economy was built.

This is what we mean by the genealogy. Not a linear history — a branching tree, where each new application grew from the same root logic, shaped by the specific pressures and needs of its moment. TLS and HTTPS grew from the need to secure web traffic at scale. Digital certificates grew from the need to verify identity across the open internet. Payment rails grew from the need to move money between strangers without physical contact. National identity systems grew from the need to prove who you are to a government system that has never met you.

Each branch is still growing. Post-quantum cryptography is the response to the threat that quantum computers pose to the mathematics the whole tree depends on. AI content provenance is the newest branch — the attempt to apply cryptographic trust to a world where any piece of content can be generated at negligible cost.

"The 1976 paper didn't just solve a problem. It opened a territory. Fifty years later, we are still mapping it."

The Present Ambassador's job is to understand that territory well enough to navigate it in real time — to walk into any community and find the branch that is most alive and relevant for the people in the room. Not always the most obvious branch. Often the most surprising one.
