B6.0 — The newest branch, and the hardest

Every branch of the genealogy traced in this handbook was shaped by a specific pressure — a new need, a new threat, a new deployment context — that forced cryptographic trust to evolve. TLS was forced by the open web. Identity was forced by digital scale. Payments were forced by the need to reach the unbanked. Digital signatures were forced by the migration of legal documents. Post-quantum was forced by a new form of computation.

AI is forcing two pressures simultaneously — and they are different enough that they need to be understood separately before they can be understood together.

🎭
Face one
AI as content generator
When anything can be synthesised convincingly — images, video, audio, text, documents — the assumption that an artifact is evidence of something that happened collapses. The burden of proof inverts: you must prove content is real, not assume it is. Cryptographic provenance is the response: signing content at the point of creation so its origin and integrity can be verified.
⚔️
Face two
AI as infrastructure attacker
AI doesn't just generate content — it attacks the systems that cryptographic trust depends on. Side-channel attacks amplified by machine learning. Social engineering at industrial scale. Biometric spoofing that defeats authentication. Vulnerability discovery accelerated by AI reasoning. And at the frontier: the possibility that sufficiently capable models find cryptographic weaknesses that human analysts have missed.
Both faces challenge the same thing: the infrastructure of trust that fifty years of cryptographic development built.

The distinction matters for how a Present Ambassador frames this with a community. The content generation threat is immediately legible to almost any audience — they have seen deepfakes, received AI-generated phishing emails, or encountered fabricated social media content. The infrastructure attack threat requires more careful framing — it is less visible but potentially more consequential, because it threatens the substrate that all the other protections rest on.

Together, they represent the most acute current challenge to the genealogy. Unlike quantum computing, which requires exotic hardware and carries substantial timeline uncertainty, both AI threats are present, accelerating, and require no technology that doesn't already exist commercially. A laptop, an API key, and sufficient motivation is all it takes.

"Quantum computing is the threat on the horizon. AI is the threat under our feet. The infrastructure it is destabilising is the same infrastructure that was supposed to protect us from it."

B6.1 — When the assumption of authenticity fails

For most of human history, a photograph was evidence. Not perfect evidence — photographs could be staged, cropped, or manipulated with effort — but evidence in a meaningful sense: something had to have happened in front of a camera for an image to exist. The same was true of audio recordings, video footage, and written documents. The cost of fabrication was high enough that the presumption of authenticity was reasonable as a default.

That presumption is gone. Generative AI has reduced the cost of producing a convincing synthetic image, audio clip, or video to approximately zero. A realistic photograph of an event that never happened, a voice recording of someone saying words they never spoke, a video of a public figure doing something they never did — all of these can be produced in minutes by anyone with access to consumer tools. The technology has crossed a threshold that cannot be uncrossed.

This is not primarily a technical problem. It is an epistemic one. Legal systems, financial markets, democratic institutions, journalism, and scientific practice all depend on the assumption that evidence corresponds to reality. When that assumption fails — when it becomes rational to doubt any digital artifact — the infrastructure of institutional trust begins to crack.

2017
Deepfakes emerge — face synthesis becomes accessible
A Reddit user publishes a technique for swapping faces in video using a deep learning model, making convincing face synthesis accessible to anyone with a consumer GPU. Within months, the technique is used for non-consensual intimate imagery at scale — one of the earliest and most harmful applications of generative synthesis. The term "deepfake" enters the vocabulary. What had required a Hollywood effects studio now requires a laptop.
2019
Voice cloning fraud — $243,000 stolen using AI audio
A UK energy company executive transfers €220,000 to a fraudulent account after receiving a phone call from a voice he believed to be his German CEO — a voice synthesised by AI. The fraud is among the first documented cases of AI voice cloning used for financial crime. The authentication system — the human ability to recognise a familiar voice — had been comprehensively defeated. This is not a future risk. It happened. It has since been replicated thousands of times at smaller scales.
2022–2023
Text generation reaches mass deployment — the document crisis
The deployment of large language models at consumer scale produces a new dimension of the content crisis: synthetic text that is indistinguishable from human writing. Fabricated news articles, synthetic academic papers, AI-generated legal documents, fraudulent financial reports, fake government communications. The volume of potentially synthetic text in circulation begins to outpace the capacity of detection systems. Courts begin encountering AI-generated evidence submitted as genuine.
2024
Video synthesis at scale — the final threshold
Real-time video synthesis — the ability to generate convincing video of a person saying or doing anything, in real time, from a webcam feed — becomes commercially available. Video calls, identity verification sessions, remote depositions, parliamentary proceedings — any context where a remote video feed is taken as evidence of a person's presence and identity is now potentially vulnerable. The authentication systems that assumed a live video feed was authentic have no technical basis for that assumption.
Now
The liar's dividend — doubt as a weapon
The most insidious consequence of the content generation crisis is not that fabricated content is believed. It is that genuine content can now be credibly denied. A politician caught on genuine video doing something damaging can claim it is a deepfake. An authentic recording of a crime can be challenged in court as potentially synthetic. A real document can be dismissed as AI-generated. The existence of sophisticated synthesis tools creates what researchers call the "liar's dividend" — the ability to weaponise doubt against genuine evidence. This is the epistemic crisis that cryptographic provenance infrastructure is being built to address.
For your community engagement
The liar's dividend is the most important concept to convey from this section — because it reframes the content crisis from "fake things will be believed" (which audiences often dismiss as a media literacy problem) to "real things can now be denied" (which affects everyone with a stake in evidence, accountability, and truth). A Present Ambassador who can explain the liar's dividend clearly has given their community a new frame for understanding why cryptographic provenance infrastructure is a public good, not a niche technical concern.
B6.2 — The cryptographic response: provenance infrastructure

The response to the content generation crisis that has gained the most institutional traction is cryptographic provenance — signing content at the point of creation so that its origin and any subsequent transformations can be verified. The leading standard is C2PA: the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity, a cross-industry initiative whose membership includes Adobe, Microsoft, Google, Sony, Nikon, Canon, the BBC, Reuters, Associated Press, and dozens of other organisations.

C2PA's architecture is a direct application of the digital signature branch of the genealogy. A camera, AI system, or publishing platform signs a piece of content — an image, a video, an audio file, a document — using a private key, creating a Content Credential: a signed record of who created the content, when, with what tool, and what transformations have been applied to it. The credential travels with the content and can be verified by anyone with access to the corresponding public key.

How a Content Credential is created and verified
The same digital signature logic from Module A, applied to the provenance of content rather than the content of a message.
📷
1 — Capture: the camera signs at the moment of creation
A C2PA-enabled camera — Sony Alpha series, Leica M11-P, Nikon Z9 — cryptographically signs the image at the moment of capture. The signature includes a hash of the raw image file, metadata about the device, GPS coordinates if available, and a timestamp. The private key is embedded in the camera's secure enclave. The camera manufacturer's CA vouches for the key through a certificate chain.
✦ Camera private key signs image hash + metadata
✂️
2 — Edit: transformations are recorded and re-signed
When the image is edited in Adobe Photoshop or Lightroom, the editing software adds a new assertion to the Content Credential — recording what was changed, by whom, and with what tool. The original signature is preserved. Each transformation adds a new signed layer. The result is a verifiable chain of custody from capture through to publication, showing every step the content has taken.
✦ Adobe signs transformation record, preserving original signature
📰
3 — Publish: the news organisation adds its assertion
When the BBC or Reuters publishes the image, their content management system adds a final assertion — signing the publication record with the organisation's certificate. The Content Credential now carries the full provenance chain: camera, editor, publisher. Each link is cryptographically verifiable. Each party is accountable for their link in the chain.
✦ Publisher signs publication assertion
🔍
4 — Verify: anyone can inspect the full provenance chain
A reader, platform, or verification service can inspect the Content Credential — available through the "cr" icon in Adobe-compatible viewers or through contentcredentials.org — and verify the full chain: the camera's signature, the editing history, the publisher's assertion. Any break in the chain — any step where the content was modified outside a signed assertion — is visible. The credential cannot prove the content is "true" — but it can prove who created it, what they did to it, and whether it has been tampered with since.
✦ Public key verification — anyone can check, nobody needs to trust an intermediary

C2PA also defines how AI-generated content should be labelled. An AI system that is C2PA-compliant — currently including Adobe Firefly, Microsoft's Designer, and others — signs its outputs with an assertion identifying the content as AI-generated. The genealogy is doing exactly what it has always done: providing a mathematical basis for a claim that the human and institutional layer cannot make on its own.

The coverage gap — the binding constraint
C2PA works when content is signed. Most content is not signed. The vast majority of images, videos, and documents in circulation have no Content Credential and never will — they were created before C2PA existed, or by tools that have not implemented it, or by people who have no reason to. This means C2PA can provide strong provenance guarantees for content created within the C2PA ecosystem — primarily professional journalism and corporate communications — while providing no signal at all for the vast ocean of unverified content where most disinformation actually travels. The absence of a credential does not prove content is fake. It proves nothing. This asymmetry — strong proof where it exists, no signal where it doesn't — is the current limitation of provenance infrastructure.
B6.3 — AI attacking the infrastructure of trust

The second face of the AI threat is less visible but in many ways more structurally dangerous. AI is not just generating content that cryptographic trust must verify — it is actively attacking the systems that cryptographic trust depends on. These attacks do not require breaking the mathematics. They target the human, institutional, and implementation layers where the genealogy has always been most vulnerable.

AI-powered social engineering — the human layer, industrialised

Social engineering — manipulating people rather than breaking cryptography — has always been the most effective attack vector against cryptographic infrastructure. A private key that has never left its hardware enclave can still be compromised if the person who holds it is tricked into using it on behalf of an attacker. AI has transformed social engineering from a skilled, labour-intensive activity into an industrialisable one.

AI-generated phishing emails are now indistinguishable from legitimate correspondence — personalised with scraped information about the target, written in their language and register, timed to their working patterns. Voice cloning allows attackers to impersonate known and trusted individuals in phone calls. Video synthesis enables real-time impersonation in video calls. Large language models can maintain a coherent fraudulent persona across extended email exchanges — something that previously required a skilled human social engineer.

The cryptographic infrastructure is intact. The authentication passed. The attack succeeded by circumventing the human who controls the keys — not by breaking the keys themselves. This is the pattern that will define AI-assisted attacks on cryptographic infrastructure for the foreseeable future: not defeating the mathematics, but defeating the humans and institutions around it.

Active threat — deployed at scale
Biometric spoofing — when your face is no longer your password

Biometric authentication — face recognition, voice recognition, liveness detection — is increasingly used as the authentication layer in national identity systems, banking apps, border control, and device unlock. The security model assumes that a biometric cannot be replicated without physical presence. AI has substantially undermined that assumption.

Face synthesis systems can generate photorealistic images and video of a person from a small number of reference photographs — all publicly available from social media. Liveness detection systems — designed to distinguish a live person from a photograph — have been defeated by 3D-printed masks, high-resolution video loops, and adversarial perturbations that fool the detection model while remaining imperceptible to humans. Voice cloning requires as little as three seconds of audio to produce a convincing replica. Remote identity verification sessions — used for KYC in banking, for university examinations, for benefits claims — are increasingly vulnerable to real-time video synthesis that substitutes a synthetic face for the genuine applicant.

The response is an arms race between increasingly sophisticated liveness detection and increasingly sophisticated spoofing — a race that the authentication systems are currently losing in several deployment contexts. Cryptographic alternatives — hardware security keys, passkeys, device-bound credentials — are phishing-resistant and spoof-resistant because they do not rely on a biological signal that can be replicated. The trajectory favors moving authentication from "something you are" toward "something you have" that is cryptographically bound to a specific device.

Active threat — documented in production systems
AI-accelerated side-channel attacks — reading secrets from physical signals

Side-channel attacks exploit information leaked by the physical implementation of a cryptographic algorithm — power consumption, electromagnetic emissions, timing variations, acoustic signals — rather than the algorithm's mathematics. A device performing a cryptographic operation emits physical signals that, with sufficient measurement and analysis, can be used to reconstruct the secret key. These attacks are real, documented, and have been used against smartcards, HSMs, and embedded cryptographic devices.

What AI has changed is the analysis layer. Classical side-channel attacks require significant expertise to extract a key from noisy physical measurements. Machine learning models — specifically deep learning applied to power traces or electromagnetic measurements — can learn to extract key material from signals that would be too noisy for classical analysis. Research groups have demonstrated AI-accelerated side-channel attacks that reduce the number of measurements required by an order of magnitude compared to classical approaches, making attacks practical against devices and in environments that were previously too difficult to exploit.

The implication is that hardware security devices — smartcards, HSMs, secure enclaves — that were designed with classical side-channel countermeasures may need reassessment against AI-assisted analysis. This is a significant concern for the payment terminals, identity documents, and signing tokens that underlie much of the infrastructure traced in B2, B3, and B4.

Emerging threat — demonstrated in research, increasingly practical
Vulnerability discovery at scale — finding what humans missed

Cryptographic security in practice depends not just on sound algorithms but on correct implementations. A correctly designed protocol implemented with a subtle coding error can be catastrophically broken — Heartbleed being the most prominent example. Finding these implementation vulnerabilities has historically required skilled human security researchers working manually through millions of lines of code.

AI-assisted vulnerability discovery — using large language models and specialised code analysis tools to identify security-relevant patterns in code — has demonstrably accelerated the rate at which vulnerabilities are found. In 2024, Google's Project Zero documented several cases where AI-assisted analysis identified memory safety vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure code that had been present for years without detection. The same capability that helps defenders find and patch vulnerabilities can be used by attackers to find and exploit them — and the attacker needs only find one; the defender must patch all of them.

For cryptographic infrastructure specifically, this means that the large corpus of legacy code underlying TLS implementations, certificate management systems, cryptographic libraries, and hardware drivers is now being analysed at a rate and scale that human researchers alone could not achieve. The vulnerability surface has not changed — but the speed at which it can be surveyed has.

Emerging threat — accelerating rapidly
AI-generated malicious code — the subtle implementation problem

AI code generation tools are increasingly used in software development — including, inevitably, in the development of security-critical code. The concern is not primarily that AI will write overtly malicious code — that is detectable. The concern is that AI tools may introduce subtle implementation errors in cryptographic code that create exploitable vulnerabilities while passing code review and automated testing.

Research has demonstrated that current large language models, when asked to implement cryptographic primitives, produce code with security-relevant errors at a higher rate than experienced human cryptographic engineers — including errors in random number generation, timing-safe comparison, padding handling, and key management that are precisely the class of mistakes that have historically produced exploitable vulnerabilities. As AI-generated code becomes more prevalent in the software supply chain, and as that code is less carefully reviewed than code written by specialists, the probability of subtle cryptographic implementation errors reaching production increases.

The code signing infrastructure from B4 — software supply chain security — is directly relevant here: a signed software package is authentic. It may still be subtly broken. Provenance and correctness are different guarantees, and AI-generated code that passes automated tests and code review may satisfy the former while failing the latter in ways that are not immediately apparent.

Emerging threat — increasingly documented
Automated credential stuffing — authentication at machine scale

Credential stuffing — using stolen username and password combinations from one breach to attack other services — is among the most prevalent and damaging attacks on authentication infrastructure. It relies on the fact that people reuse passwords across services. Its effectiveness has always been limited by the rate at which an automated system can attempt logins before triggering rate limiting or detection.

AI has substantially raised the ceiling on what automated attacks can achieve. Machine learning models trained on CAPTCHA-solving, behavioural mimicry, and account takeover patterns can defeat the friction-based defences that most services deploy — passing bot detection, mimicking human browsing patterns, solving visual challenges, and adapting to defensive measures in near-real time. AI-powered credential stuffing operations now operate at the scale of millions of attempts per hour across distributed infrastructure, with automated adaptation to defensive countermeasures.

The cryptographic response — phishing-resistant authentication through passkeys and hardware security keys — is the only reliable defence, because it replaces the shared secret (the password that can be stuffed) with a cryptographic proof that is bound to a specific device. Platforms that have deployed passkeys see credential stuffing attacks drop to near zero for enrolled users. The genealogy provides the solution — but only where it has been deployed.

Active threat — operating at industrial scale
Case study — for community engagement
The £25 million video call
In January 2024, a finance worker at a multinational company in Hong Kong transferred HK$200 million — approximately £25 million — to fraudulent accounts after attending a video call in which every other participant, including a person he believed to be the company's CFO, was a deepfake. The worker had initially been suspicious of a phishing email requesting the transfer, but his doubts were allayed by the video call, in which the synthesised participants appeared entirely convincing. He made fifteen transactions before the fraud was discovered. No cryptographic authentication was broken. The attack defeated the human's judgement entirely through the synthesis of convincing video. This case is not an outlier — it is a demonstration of a class of attack that is now well within the capability of moderately resourced criminal organisations, and that will become more accessible as real-time synthesis tools improve. For any community that conducts significant financial or legal business over video calls, the question is not whether this could happen but what authentication infrastructure exists that would detect it.
B6.4 — At the edge of what we know

The attack vectors in Tab 3 are documented, active, and addressable with known countermeasures. This tab addresses something different: the frontier — the questions about AI and cryptographic trust that the field is genuinely uncertain about, where the honest answer requires holding the uncertainty rather than resolving it prematurely.

A Present Ambassador who reaches the frontier in a community conversation should do so with intellectual honesty: these are not settled questions, the expert community is actively debating them, and the uncertainty itself is information worth sharing.

Frontier question 1
Can sufficiently capable AI models discover new cryptographic attacks?
This is the question that the Mythos reference points toward. A sufficiently capable model — trained on the full corpus of cryptographic literature, capable of novel mathematical reasoning, able to explore large search spaces faster than human researchers — might find attack vectors that human cryptanalysts have missed. This is not idle speculation: SIKE was broken by human insight that its designers did not anticipate. The question is whether AI-assisted mathematical reasoning could find a comparable insight in a currently trusted algorithm faster than the human community would find it through conventional research.

The honest answer is: we don't know. The cryptographic community has not reached consensus on how to assess this risk, how to test for it, or how to incorporate it into security assumptions. What can be said is that the assumption "no attack is currently known, therefore no attack exists" becomes less reliable as the capability of AI reasoning tools increases. This is the provable/conjectured distinction from B5, applied to AI rather than quantum computers — and it is the reason that responsible cryptographic governance is moving toward mathematical diversity and formal verification rather than relying solely on the absence of known attacks.
Frontier question 2
What does AI-assisted cryptanalysis mean for the post-quantum transition?
The NIST post-quantum standards were evaluated by human cryptanalysts over eight years. The security confidence placed in CRYSTALS-Kyber and CRYSTALS-Dilithium rests on the failure of that community to find efficient attacks. But the evaluation was conducted in a world before highly capable AI reasoning tools were widely available. The question now being asked by some researchers is whether the standards should be re-evaluated — not by human cryptanalysts alone, but with AI-assisted analysis — before critical infrastructure is migrated to them.

This is not a mainstream position, and most cryptographers consider the lattice-based standards to be well-founded. But it is a live research question, and it represents the intersection of two of the most significant challenges in the field: post-quantum migration and AI capability. A Present Ambassador should be aware that these two branches of the genealogy are not independent — they interact in ways that the field is still working to understand.
Frontier question 3
Who governs AI-assisted provenance infrastructure?
C2PA provides a technical standard for content provenance. But the trust infrastructure that gives C2PA its meaning — the Certificate Authorities that vouch for camera manufacturers and publishing organisations, the platforms that display Content Credentials, the regulators that may eventually require them — is governed by a small number of large technology companies and standards bodies. The question of who controls the provenance infrastructure of global media is not a technical question. It is a political one.

In contexts where the state controls the Certificate Authorities that vouch for content provenance, C2PA could become a mechanism for state authentication of approved content — inverting its purpose entirely. In contexts where platform companies control the display of Content Credentials, the decision of whose credentials to display and whose to suppress becomes a form of editorial power. The genealogy has always been subject to this dynamic: mathematical trust is only as good as the institutions that administer it. Content provenance infrastructure inherits that limitation.
Frontier question 4
Can we build AI systems that are cryptographically accountable?
The deepest long-term question at the intersection of AI and cryptographic trust is whether the outputs of AI systems can be made verifiably accountable — not just labelled, but cryptographically provable as to their origin, the model that generated them, the training data they reflect, and the constraints they were operating under. This is the research agenda of fields including zero-knowledge proofs applied to machine learning, verifiable computation, and model transparency.

It is technically demanding and not yet practically deployable at scale. But it represents the most ambitious application of the genealogy to the AI problem: not just signing content after the fact, but building AI systems whose operation is cryptographically auditable from the inside. If the genealogy continues its pattern of expanding to meet each new trust challenge, this is where it leads next.
How to hold the frontier in a community conversation
The frontier questions do not have answers to deliver. What a Present Ambassador can offer is something more valuable: the frame. "Here is what is known. Here is what is uncertain. Here is why the uncertainty matters, and who is working on it." A community that learns to hold that frame — rather than demanding false certainty in either direction — is better equipped to engage with the decisions being made about their digital infrastructure than one that receives a simplified narrative of threats and solutions. The genealogy is not finished. It is being written right now, in part by the people in the room.
B6.5 — AI and trust in your community

Every community is already inside the AI and cryptographic trust story — most without knowing it. The question for a Present Ambassador is where the story is most present, most consequential, and most in need of the understanding that only someone who has traced the genealogy can provide.

1
Find where the content crisis has already arrived
In almost every community, AI-generated content has already caused confusion, damage, or both. Election-related deepfakes. Synthetic voice fraud. AI-generated news in local media. Fabricated evidence submitted in legal proceedings. Fake credentials or academic work. Find the specific instance in your community — not a global example, but a local one. The specificity is what makes the problem real rather than theoretical for the people in the room.
2
Map the attack surface in your community's infrastructure
Which organisations in your community are most exposed to the AI attack vectors in Tab 3? Financial institutions and payment operators: credential stuffing, voice fraud, video call fraud. Identity verification services: biometric spoofing. Government agencies: AI-assisted social engineering of staff with privileged access. Healthcare: synthetic patient records and fraudulent insurance claims. Legal institutions: AI-generated evidence. Map the exposure — then ask whether the organisations responsible know they are exposed.

Which media organisations, government agencies, or official information sources in your community have deployed C2PA or Content Credentials? The answer in most communities outside major Western news organisations is: none. That gap is informative — and it is the entry point for a conversation about what it would take to build provenance infrastructure for content that your community depends on for democratic, legal, or economic purposes.

Find the entry point for your specific audience
For a general public audience: the liar's dividend — the fact that authentic content can now be credibly denied. For a media and journalism audience: C2PA adoption and the coverage gap, and what a commitment to signed content would mean for their credibility. For a legal or policy audience: the implications of AI-generated evidence and deepfake denial for evidentiary standards. For a security and technology audience: the AI attack vectors against authentication infrastructure and the case for phishing-resistant cryptographic authentication. For any audience: the Hong Kong video call fraud — a single case study that makes both the content generation threat and the AI attacker threat vivid simultaneously.
