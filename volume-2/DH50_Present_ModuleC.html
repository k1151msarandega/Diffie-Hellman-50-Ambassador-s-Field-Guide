C.0 — A different kind of question

Module B asked: how does this branch of the genealogy work, in general — what pressures shaped it, what does it make possible, where has it been stressed? Those are questions about the world. Module C asks a different kind of question: what does this branch actually look like, right now, in the specific place where you will be standing when you walk into a room?

The difference matters. "TLS secures the web" is true everywhere. "The three government services my community uses most have TLS grades of B, C, and F, and the F is the one that handles tax filings" is true only here — and it is the kind of fact that changes a conversation. Module B gave you the vocabulary and the genealogical fluency. Module C is where you turn that fluency into a map of one specific terrain: yours.

"The genealogy tells you what is possible. The landscape tells you what is actually here. A Present Ambassador needs both — but a community only experiences the second."

This module produces a single deliverable: a Living Landscape Brief — a survey of cryptographic trust as it currently stands in your region, organised around the branches you've already traced. It is not exhaustive. It is not a formal audit. It is the picture that lets you walk into any room in your community and answer, with specifics, the question: "what does this actually look like here?"

This feeds the global picture too
The "current state of DPI" reports mentioned in the DH50 Present Ambassador brief — country and regional surveys of digital public infrastructure — are exactly what a completed Living Landscape Brief contributes to. Your brief, alongside those of ambassadors in other regions, becomes part of a global picture of where cryptographic trust infrastructure stands in 2026: what's deployed, what's working, what's missing, and where the gaps are sharpest.

You don't need green across the board
A landscape with gaps, absences, and "unknowns" is not a failed brief — it is an honest one, and often more useful than a brief that found everything. The gaps themselves are information: they show you, and your community, where the genealogy hasn't reached yet, and they're often exactly where the most productive conversations happen.
C.2 — How to fill the map

The research approach here builds directly on what B7 already taught you about working in environments where documentation is uneven. The difference is scope: B7 asked you to go deep on one branch. C asks you to go wide across seven — which means triage, prioritisation, and accepting that some cells of your map will be thinner than others.

1
Start from what you already have
Your log entries from Modules A through B7 already contain partial answers to many of the Tab 1 questions. Pull them together first. This gives you a baseline and shows you immediately where the real gaps are — which is where your research time should go.
2
Use the tools named across B1–B6
Several modules pointed to specific tools: SSL Labs' Server Test for TLS grading (B1), official government digital strategy publications (B2, B5), central bank and payment regulator websites (B3), national electronic signature registries (B4). Revisit these — they're directly applicable here, now applied systematically across your priority services rather than one example.
3
Prioritise the services your community actually uses
You don't need to map every system in your country — you need to map the systems that matter to the people you'll be engaging. If your audience is a local council, prioritise municipal services. If it's a university community, prioritise education and research infrastructure. The map should be wide enough to be honest, but focused enough to be useful.
4
Treat silence as data — but verify it's real silence
As B7 noted, the absence of information can itself be informative. But before marking a cell "unknown," make sure you've checked the obvious places: has anyone published a national digital strategy, a central bank fintech roadmap, an e-government progress report? International bodies (World Bank, ITU, GSMA) often publish country-level digital infrastructure assessments that cover gaps national sources don't.
5
Look for the surprises
As you fill in the map, watch for the cells that surprise you — a branch that's far more advanced than you expected, or far less. Surprises are often the strongest material for community engagement, because they're surprising to your audience too. A government tax portal with an F-grade TLS configuration is a more compelling opening than a general statement about cybersecurity.
This is a living document, deliberately
"The Living Landscape" is named that way for a reason — the picture you build now will be out of date within months in some areas (a new payment rail launches, a post-quantum policy is announced) and stable for years in others. Treat the brief as a snapshot you can update, not a final report. Note the date you researched each section, so future updates — by you or by another ambassador — know what's current and what needs revisiting.
