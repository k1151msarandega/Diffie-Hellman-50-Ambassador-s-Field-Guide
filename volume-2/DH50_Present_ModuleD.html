Before you walk in
D.0 — The temptation to skip ahead

By this point, you know a great deal. You've traced the genealogy of cryptographic trust across six major branches and one of your own. You've mapped the living landscape of your region — what's deployed, what's missing, what's contested. You are, by any reasonable measure, fluent. The natural impulse at this point is to start building: a workshop outline, a talk structure, a slide deck. The craft modules are right there, waiting.

This module asks you to pause before that — not because the craft doesn't matter, but because everything the craft modules teach you assumes something this module provides: a clear picture of who you're actually talking to.

Here is the problem with skipping ahead. Module A through C gave you a vast amount of material — branches, case studies, statistics, frameworks, comparisons. Without a clear picture of your specific audience, the temptation when building an engagement is to include as much of it as possible, because all of it feels important. The result is a workshop or talk that tries to cover everything and lands nothing. The Present Ambassador's job, as established back in Module 0, is not to transmit information — it is to create a moment of recognition. That moment requires choosing the right entry point for the right audience, and that choice cannot be made well until the audience is known.

"You now hold a map of the entire territory. This module is where you ask: who, exactly, am I inviting to look at it with me — and what part of the map will mean something to them?"

Recall Module 0's four profiles — the educator, the practitioner, the community organiser, the communicator. Each has a different relationship to "their community." For some, the community is a given — a practitioner working in digital identity already has a professional community they're embedded in. For others, the community is a choice — an educator might engage their existing students, or might choose to run a session for a community group they're connected to outside work. Either way, the work of this module is the same: turning "my community" from a category into a specific, known group of people.

The output of this module
A short Community Profile — shorter than your Living Landscape Brief, but just as essential. It captures who your audience is, what they already trust and use, what's invisible to them, and what entry point connects your fluency to their lived experience. Module E will ask you to design an engagement around this profile. Without it, Module E has nothing to design around.
D.1 — Four dimensions of knowing a community

"Know your audience" is true and unhelpful as stated — it doesn't tell you what to actually find out. The four dimensions below translate it into specific questions, each connected to the fluency you've already built. Select each to see what it asks and why it matters.

🤝
What they already trust, use, and depend on
Every community has a set of digital tools, services, and systems woven into daily life — often without anyone thinking of them as "cryptographic infrastructure" at all. A banking app. A government portal. A messaging platform. A payment method. These are your raw material: the things from your Living Landscape Brief that are not abstract to this audience, because they touch it every day. Before you can show someone something invisible, you need to know what's visible to them — what they already use, without question, every day.
😟
What they fear, distrust, or have been let down by
Distrust is often a more productive starting point than trust, because it's where people are already paying attention. Has this community experienced a scam, a fraud, a data breach, a service outage, an exclusion from a benefit due to authentication failure? These experiences — even if the community doesn't connect them to "cryptography" — are exactly the entry points the genealogy can illuminate. Module B's stress points (DigiNotar, Aadhaar exclusions, APP fraud, the Hong Kong deepfake call) all map onto experiences that ordinary communities have, in smaller and more local forms.
👁️
What's most invisible to them — and why
Invisibility isn't uniform. Some infrastructure is invisible because it works perfectly (TLS, for most people, most of the time). Some is invisible because it's deliberately obscured (who controls a payment rail, who issued a certificate). Some is invisible because the community has never had a reason to look (post-quantum migration, AI content provenance). Identifying which kind of invisibility applies to which part of your Living Landscape Brief, for this specific audience, tells you what kind of "reveal" will land — a "did you know this was happening underneath something you do every day?" moment, versus a "did you know this decision was being made, and you weren't told?" moment.
🔗
Their relationship to the infrastructure — user, excluded, builder, regulator
A room of people who use a system, a room of people excluded from it, a room of people who build it, and a room of people who regulate it are four different audiences — even if the system under discussion is the same. A workshop for users might focus on "here's what's happening when you do this." A session with people excluded by a system might focus on "here's why this happens to you, and here's the choice that caused it." A session with builders or regulators might focus on "here's the trade-off you're making, and here's how other regions have made it differently." Knowing which relationship (or mix of relationships) is in the room shapes everything about tone, framing, and what you're actually offering them.
1
For ambassadors with an existing community
If you're a practitioner, educator, or organiser already embedded in a community — your students, your colleagues, your professional network, your local organisation — your task is to look at that community freshly through these four dimensions. You may know them well personally, but have you ever specifically asked what they trust, fear, can't see, or how they relate to the infrastructure you now understand so well?
2
For ambassadors choosing a community
If you're choosing who to engage — perhaps from several possible communities you have access to — let these four dimensions guide the choice itself. A community where you can answer all four questions with specificity, because you already have real relationships there, will almost always make for a stronger engagement than a community you have only general or theoretical access to. Proximity and relationship are themselves part of the "fluency" this handbook has been building toward.
Activity — 20 minutes
Apply the four dimensions

Choose the community you intend to engage — or, if undecided, the community you're currently leaning toward. Work through each of the four dimensions above, writing down what you know and, honestly, what you don't.

  • For anything you don't know, is it something you could find out by simply asking someone in that community — a quick conversation rather than formal research?
  • Does anything you've written down connect directly to a specific finding from your Living Landscape Brief (Module C)? If so, that connection may be your entry point.
D.2 — Building your community profile

The template below is deliberately short. Unlike the Living Landscape Brief, which rewards thoroughness, the Community Profile rewards specificity and honesty — a few sentences per section that are precisely true are worth more than a page of generalities.

Who is the community
Be as specific as possible. Not "young people" — "second-year students in the computer science club at [institution]." Not "the public" — "residents attending the monthly ward council meeting in [neighbourhood]."
Your relationship to this community
How do you know them, or how would you gain access? What credibility or trust do you already have with them — and what would you need to build?
What they already trust, use, and depend on
The specific tools, services, and systems woven into this community's daily life.
What they fear, distrust, or have been let down by
Specific experiences — a scam, an outage, an exclusion, a scandal — that this community has lived through or talks about.
What's most invisible to them — and which kind of invisible
Working perfectly and unnoticed? Deliberately obscured? Never had reason to look? Which part of your Living Landscape Brief does this connect to?
Their relationship to the infrastructure
User, excluded, builder, regulator — or some mix. What does that mean for tone and framing?
The entry point
Drawing on everything above — and on the entry point you identified in your Living Landscape Brief — what is the single opening that connects your fluency to this community's lived experience?
If the entry point doesn't come easily
That's a signal worth listening to — not a failure. It may mean the community needs more thought, or a conversation with someone in it before you proceed, or that a different community (one you have a clearer line of sight into) would serve better for a first engagement. There's no requirement to use the first community you consider. Module E goes more smoothly when this module produces genuine clarity rather than a forced answer.
