Module B1 — Before you go looking

The most important thing you will do before any investigation is frame the question you are actually trying to answer.

Not "what is the history of cryptography in my country" — that is a topic, not a question. A question has a specific answer that either exists or doesn't, and that you can go looking for. A good investigative question points you toward the sources you need.

"I want to find out who built the first system in my country that used digital signatures — and what made them decide to do it when they did."

That question has named people in it, a specific technology, a time period, and a human motivation. Each of those is a thread you can pull. Compare it to: "I want to understand how cryptography developed in my country." That question has nowhere to start.

How to frame your question
1
Start with what you already know
Go back to your Module A bridge log entry. You found a trace — a system, an institution, a name. That trace is the starting point of your question. Your question should be specific enough that answering it would require you to find at least one person or document you don't currently have access to.
2
Identify what you don't know — and what kind of source would tell you
For every gap in your knowledge, ask: is this something that would be in a document (policy, technical specification, meeting minutes, news report), or something that only a person would know? Documents and people require different search strategies.
3
Ask whether your question is primary or secondary
A secondary question can be answered by reading what others have written. A primary question requires you to find sources that haven't been synthesised before — interviews, unpublished documents, institutional records. The best History Ambassador work lives in primary territory. Use secondary sources to orient, then go further.
4
Check that your question has a local dimension
Your contribution to the global picture is the specific story from where you are. If your question could be answered from London or San Francisco without anyone ever going to your community, it isn't the right question. The right question requires your presence, your language, your relationships.
The question you can't answer yet is the right question
If you already know the answer, it isn't an investigative question — it's a summary. A good question should make you slightly uncomfortable, because it points toward something genuinely unknown. That discomfort is the engine of real fieldwork.

The two labs in this module walk you through the source-finding process for two specific, real deployments — one historical, one current. In each lab, you will practice the full cycle: identifying what you need to know, locating the sources that would tell you, and mapping the gaps.

Then you apply the same process to your own investigation.

Lab 1 — Historical deployment
Singapore Network Services & TradeNet, 1987–1989
The world's first nationwide electronic trade documentation system — and the cryptographic trust decisions buried inside it.
How this lab works: You are not being told what to find. You are being walked through how to look. The sources listed here are real and publicly accessible. Your job is to follow the trail, note what you find, and identify what's missing. The answers exist — but you have to retrieve them.
The scenario

In January 1989, Singapore launched TradeNet — the world's first nationwide electronic single window for trade documentation. Within a year, it was handling nearly half of all trade declarations for the port. By 1991, the government mandated its use for all trade transactions.

Behind that headline is a specific story: a small group of people, at a specific institution, made decisions about how to build trust into an electronic system at a time when no standard playbook existed. Who were they? What did they decide? And what cryptographic choices did they make — or inherit from the systems they integrated with?

Your investigation has three layers: the institution, the people, and the technology.

The source map — start here

Below are the categories of sources available for this investigation, with specific starting points for each. Work through them in order — each layer will refine your questions for the next.

Archive
National Library Board Singapore — eResources History Portal
Singapore's NLB maintains a curated history record for TradeNet, including digitised newspaper coverage from the Straits Times and Business Times going back to 1986. Named individuals, government statements, and contemporaneous reporting are all available. Search "TradeNet" in the NLB newspaper archive.
eresources.nlb.gov.sg
Document
NLB History Event Record — "TradeNet is officially launched"
A structured history record citing primary sources, naming key figures including Philip Yeo (then chairman of the National Computer Board) and describing the institutional structure of Singapore Network Services Pte Ltd, the joint-venture company that built and operated TradeNet.
eresources.nlb.gov.sg/history/events/8db3ea66-c99a-41a0-bd9a-b6300d1a4454
Person
Philip Yeo — National Computer Board chairman, 1981–1986
One of the principal architects of Singapore's early digital infrastructure. Has given interviews and spoken publicly about the National Computer Board's work in this period. Search for interviews or published accounts — he is a named, findable figure with a documented public record.
Document
CrimsonLogic case study — TradeNet
CrimsonLogic (formerly Singapore Network Services) maintains a published case study of the TradeNet system describing its technical and institutional history. Useful as a secondary source to cross-reference against primary reporting.
crimsonlogic.com/case-study-singapore-tradenet
Archive
Academic studies — ERIA and WCO documentation
TradeNet has been the subject of multiple academic and policy studies cited in the ERIA (Economic Research Institute for ASEAN and East Asia) and World Customs Organization documentation. These contain citations to primary government documents and can point toward further sources.
The investigation — work through these in order
Activity B1.1 — The institution
Map who built TradeNet and why

Singapore Network Services Pte Ltd was a joint venture between three organisations. Find out which three, when the joint venture was established, and what mandate each partner brought to it.

  • Who initiated the project — was it government-led, industry-led, or a response to external pressure?
  • What was the National Computer Board's role, and how does that compare to how similar projects are governed in your own country?
  • What was the timeline from decision to launch — and what does that timeline reveal about the institutional capacity Singapore had in this period?

Where to look: NLB history record, contemporaneous newspaper coverage in the NLB archive (search "TradeNet" and "National Computer Board" in the Straits Times, 1986–1989).

Activity B1.2 — The people
Find the named individuals and trace their decisions

Philip Yeo is named in the historical record. He is not the only person who made TradeNet happen — but he is a starting point. From his name, work outward.

  • What specifically did Philip Yeo say about TradeNet in contemporaneous statements? Find at least one direct quote from 1986–1989, not a later retrospective.
  • Who else is named in the NLB newspaper archive in connection with TradeNet? Build a short list of the people who appear most frequently — these are your primary targets if you were conducting this investigation for real.
  • Are any of these people findable today — still active, with a public profile, potentially reachable for an interview?

Technique note: When reading archived newspaper articles, note not just who is quoted but who else is mentioned by name, and in what context. The people quoted in press releases are rarely the ones who made the hardest decisions.

Activity B1.3 — The technology
What cryptographic choices did TradeNet make — and where are the gaps?

TradeNet was built before SSL existed and before digital signatures were standardised. The electronic data interchange (EDI) protocols it used had their own security model. This is where the investigation gets genuinely difficult — and genuinely interesting.

  • What EDI standard did TradeNet use? What security mechanisms did that standard include in 1989?
  • How did TradeNet verify that a trade declaration was authentic — that it came from who it claimed to come from, and hadn't been altered in transit?
  • What does the public record tell you about this — and where does it go silent? The silence is part of your finding.

Note on gaps: You may not be able to answer the technology questions from public sources alone. That is intentional. In your own investigation, this is the moment you would need to find a person — an engineer, a technologist, someone who was in the room. Note who that person might be for TradeNet, even if you can't reach them.

Lab 2 — Current deployment
Philippine Statistics Authority & PhilSys, 2018–present
A national digital ID system built on public-key cryptography — and a society that didn't immediately trust it.
Why this case: PhilSys is a live deployment with living sources. The people who built it are reachable. The people who resisted it are documented. And the story of how a society learns to trust a new form of cryptographic identity is still unfolding. This is what your own investigation may look like.
The scenario

In August 2018, the Philippines signed Republic Act 11055 — the Philippine Identification System Act — establishing a single national ID system for all citizens and resident aliens. The system uses asymmetric public-key cryptography embedded in a QR code on the physical card, enabling digital verification of identity without a handwritten signature.

When the cards were issued, banks refused to accept them. The absence of a handwritten signature made the cards unrecognisable as legitimate identity documents to institutions whose verification processes had been built around a different trust model. The Philippine Statistics Authority had to issue memorandums instructing banks to accept their own government's ID.

"The mathematics said the card was authentic. The bank teller said it wasn't. Both were, in their own way, right — about different things."

This is a story about what cryptographic trust deployment actually looks like on the ground: not a clean technical rollout, but a negotiation between a new system of verification and the human institutions that had to learn to use it.

The source map
Document
Republic Act 11055 — Philippine Identification System Act (2018)
The founding legislation. Available in full on the Official Gazette of the Philippines. Read Section 5 (authentication methods) and the Implementing Rules and Regulations for the technical framework. This is a primary legal document — read it as a source, not a summary of it.
officialgazette.gov.ph — search "Republic Act 11055"
Archive
Philippine Statistics Authority — PhilSys press releases and advisories
The PSA maintains a public archive of press releases, public advisories, and policy statements. The 2021 advisory on the non-inclusion of handwritten signatures is particularly relevant — it documents the bank resistance problem directly, in the PSA's own voice.
philsys.gov.ph and psa.gov.ph
Person
National Statistician — PSA leadership, 2018–present
The National Statistician has made public statements about PhilSys at multiple points. Named officials have given press interviews and appeared before congressional hearings. Congressional records (available through the Philippine Congress official website) are a primary source for how the system was debated and defended publicly.
Press
Philippine news archive — Rappler, Inquirer, BusinessWorld
The bank resistance story was covered in detail by Philippine business and technology press. Search "PhilID banks signature" and "PSA memorandum PhilID" in the Rappler and Inquirer archives. This coverage names the institutions that resisted and the officials who responded.
Document
NEDA — PhilSys project overview and implementation documentation
The National Economic and Development Authority maintains project documentation on PhilSys including the original systems integrator information memorandum — a detailed technical and institutional document about how the system was designed to work.
neda.gov.ph/philsys
The investigation
Activity B1.4 — The technology, made concrete
Understand exactly what the QR code does

The PhilID uses asymmetric public-key cryptography in its QR code. This is Diffie-Hellman's intellectual descendant doing a specific, practical job. Before you investigate the institutional story, make sure you understand the technical claim being made.

  • What information is encoded in the PhilID QR code, and how does the digital signature work? Read the PSA's public advisory on security features — it describes the mechanism in plain language.
  • What does "the signature is valid" mean in this context — valid according to whom, using what verification infrastructure?
  • What would it take to forge a PhilID — and how does the cryptographic design make that hard?
Activity B1.5 — The resistance
Find the bank refusal story in primary sources

The fact that banks refused to accept PhilIDs is documented. Your job is to find primary documentation of that resistance — not a summary of it, but the actual record.

  • Find the PSA advisory that addresses the bank refusal problem. What exactly does it say — who is it addressed to, what does it require, and what reason does it give?
  • Find at least one contemporaneous news report that names a specific bank or banking association that raised objections. What was their stated reason?
  • What does the resistance reveal about the gap between a technically sound system and an institutionally accepted one? This gap is the heart of the story.

Reflection: The banks were not wrong to be cautious — they were operating on a different model of trust. The PSA was not wrong to push back — the system was cryptographically sound. How do you adjudicate between two institutions that are each right about different things?

Activity B1.6 — The living sources
Identify who you would interview — and how you would reach them

Unlike TradeNet, the people who built and fought over PhilSys are alive and in many cases still in their roles. This is what your own investigation will look like. Practice the source-identification process here.

  • Name three categories of people whose perspective on PhilSys would give you different parts of the story: a builder, a resister, and someone who was excluded from or underserved by the system.
  • For each category: where would you look to find a specific individual? (Congressional records, LinkedIn, university faculty pages, civil society organisations, community leaders?)
  • What is the single most important question you would ask each of them — and why that question, rather than a more general one?
A note on current deployments and sensitivity
PhilSys is a live system affecting real people. Some aspects of its implementation — data security, vendor contracts, exclusion of marginalised communities — are politically sensitive in the Philippines. When investigating current deployments in your own region, be aware that sources may have institutional reasons to present a particular narrative. Seek multiple perspectives, especially from communities who experienced the system differently from its designers.

Module B1 — When the standard toolkit doesn't work

Not every investigation happens in a place with well-funded archives, freedom of information laws, and sources who answer emails from strangers.

The methods in Labs 1 and 2 assumed a certain infrastructure: digitised newspaper archives, institutional records online, named officials with public profiles. In many parts of the world — and for many of the most important stories in this investigation — that infrastructure doesn't exist. The trail doesn't go cold because the story isn't there. It goes cold because the story was never written down, or was written in a language that wasn't digitised, or was held in an institution that no longer exists, or by a person who doesn't know they hold it.

This is not a deficit. It is a different kind of investigation — and in many ways a more important one, because these are exactly the stories that disappear if no one goes looking.

Strategies for scarce-archive contexts
1
Go outside government channels
When formal archives are poorly funded, unindexed, or damaged, look for history in private hands: family records, institutional archives of religious organisations, colonial or trade enterprise records, NGO project documentation. The history of technology adoption often lives in the records of the organisations that funded or resisted it, not the ones that built it.
2
Treat oral sources as primary records
In contexts where the paper trail is thin, community memory is not a supplement to the historical record — it is the historical record. An engineer who deployed the first banking encryption system in your country in 1997 holds primary evidence that exists nowhere else. Cross-reference multiple oral testimonies against each other the way you would cross-reference documents. Look for where they converge and where they diverge — both are informative.
3
Build trust before asking questions
In communities where outside inquiry has historically been exploitative or politically dangerous, trust is not given — it is earned through presence and relationship. Don't arrive and expect honest answers. Arrive, be introduced through existing networks, explain your purpose clearly, and return. The best History Ambassador interviews will happen on a second or third visit, not the first.
4
Navigate the paywall problem
Academic papers about your country's technology history may be locked behind expensive Western paywalls. Contact the authors directly — most academics will share their papers with someone doing legitimate research. Reach out to university libraries for interlibrary loans. Use open-access databases. The history of your community is not inaccessible — it is just not free.
5
Know when the absence is the story
Sometimes the most important finding is that the record doesn't exist — that a decision was made but never documented, that a community was affected but never consulted, that a system was deployed without any public accountability trail. Documenting the absence, rigorously and specifically, is itself a contribution. "No record exists of X, despite Y and Z" is a finding.
A note on digital security in your investigation
In some regions, investigating who controlled or restricted access to cryptographic tools — or who benefits from continued opacity about digital infrastructure — can attract unwanted attention. If your investigation touches on politically sensitive territory, there are resources specifically designed to help History Ambassadors work safely. Your regional coordinator can connect you with these materials.
Reflection
Which of these strategies applies to your investigation?

Be honest about the specific obstacles your context presents. The ambassador in Lagos is not at a disadvantage compared to the ambassador in Singapore — they are investigating a different kind of story, with different methods. Both are necessary.

  • Which of the five strategies above is most relevant to your investigation right now?
  • What specific step will you take in the next two weeks to work around the most significant obstacle you face?
  • Is there a person — not a document — who holds the key piece of your local story? If so: who introduced you to them, or who could?
