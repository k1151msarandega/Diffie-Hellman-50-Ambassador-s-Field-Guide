Module B2 — The discipline of preparation

The most important work of an interview happens before you sit down with anyone.

A well-prepared interviewer knows what is already documented — which means they know what isn't. They don't ask a pioneer to explain things that are in every textbook. They ask about the moment before the breakthrough, the decision that almost didn't happen, the person who helped and was never credited, the thing they would do differently. Those questions can only come from someone who has done the reading.

"The well-prepared interviewer will know what information is already in documents and will use the oral history interview to seek new information, clarification, or new interpretation of a historical event."
— Smithsonian Institution Archives, How to Do Oral History

This is not just technique — it is respect. The people you will interview have told their surface-level story many times. What they haven't been asked are the questions that require someone to know them well enough to ask. That is the standard you are working toward.

Preparation in five steps
1
Map what's already documented
Before you approach anyone, find out what they have already said publicly. Interviews, papers, public statements, oral histories. Read or watch as much as you can find. Make a list of topics they have addressed thoroughly — these are off your question list. What remains is your territory.
2
Identify the undocumented gaps
As you read, note the moments where the account goes vague, where the timeline skips, where other people are mentioned but not quoted, where you sense something is being left out. These are your richest targets. Not because you are looking for secrets, but because oral history lives in the detail that never made it into the formal record.
3
Prepare specific questions, not topics
"Tell me about your work in cryptography" is a topic. "You've described the moment you realised the key exchange problem was solvable — but I couldn't find anywhere that you described who you told first, and what their reaction was" is a question. Specific questions show preparation and earn specific answers.
4
Prepare your opening and your close
The first question sets the tone. It should be open enough to let the person speak freely, but specific enough to show you're not starting from zero. The close should invite anything you haven't thought to ask: "Is there something about this story that you've never been asked, that you think matters?" That question often produces the most important answer in the interview.
5
Remember that people's views evolve
What someone said in a 1991 interview is not necessarily what they believe in 2025. People revise their understanding of their own history as time passes, as new evidence emerges, as the stakes change. When you sit with someone, you are capturing how they understand their story now — which is itself historical data, distinct from what they said before. Both are true. Neither cancels the other.
The preparation rule for this handbook's work
Before you approach anyone in your local investigation, you must be able to answer: "What do I know about what this person has already said or written?" If the answer is "nothing," you are not ready to interview them. If the answer is "I've found everything I can and I know where the gaps are," you are.
Lab 3 — Studying real interviews
Diffie, Hellman, and Merkle — in their own words
Three pioneers. Multiple interviewers. Decades of public record. Your job is to read it, map what's there, and find what isn't.

The three people at the center of this anniversary have given substantive recorded interviews across several decades. These are primary sources — not summaries, not Wikipedia entries, but the actual accounts in their own voices. They are also, collectively, a masterclass in what skilled and less-skilled interviewers produce.

Study them as both content and method. What did the interviewers ask that produced something new? What questions were missed? Where does the account go thin — and what question might have opened it up?

The lab — study the record before you question it
Activity B2.1 — Map what's already there
Read at least one interview from each person — then build your coverage map

For each of the three figures, read at least one of the interviews listed above. As you read, track which topics are well-covered and which go thin. You are building a map of what the existing record contains — so you can see clearly what it doesn't.

  • What specific moments or decisions are described in detail across multiple interviews — things each person has clearly told many times?
  • Where does an account go vague, skip over time, or mention someone else without elaborating? These are the gaps.
  • Are there moments where Diffie, Hellman, and Merkle describe the same event differently? What does the difference reveal?
  • What does the interviewer's preparation level visibly affect? Where does a good question produce a rich answer — and where does a thin question produce a thin one?
Activity B2.2 — Study the interviewer, not just the subject
What makes a question open something up?

Across these interviews, some questions produce remarkable answers and some produce nothing. Find one example of each from the transcripts you read.

A question that opened something up
What made it work? Was it specific? Did it show the interviewer knew something the subject didn't expect them to know? Did it ask about a person rather than a concept?
A question that produced little
Why did it fall flat? Was it too broad? Did it invite a well-rehearsed answer? Did it ask for a definition rather than an experience?

Write down both examples in your log. This is technique analysis — the kind that will directly improve your own interviews.

Activity B2.3 — Compare accounts of the same moment
How Diffie and Hellman each remember the collaboration

Both Diffie and Hellman have described how they began working together and how the key exchange idea developed. Their accounts are not identical. This is not because one of them is wrong — it is because memory is subjective, and two people in the same room experience it differently.

  • Find a specific moment or claim that appears in both Diffie's and Hellman's accounts. Where do they agree? Where do they diverge?
  • How does Hellman describe Merkle's contribution in his 1991 interview versus his 2004 one? What changed in how he tells that story — and what might explain the shift?
  • What does Merkle say about the moment his professor rejected his class project? Does either Diffie or Hellman mention this in their accounts? What does the silence tell you?

Contradiction between sources is not a problem to resolve. It is information about how history is made and remembered. Document it carefully.

Module B2 — The question only you can ask

You have now read what the existing record contains. You know what has been asked and answered. The question worth asking is the one that hasn't been.

This is not a hypothetical exercise. Outstanding questions submitted through this handbook will be considered for a live Q&A session with Diffie, Hellman, and Merkle as part of the DH50 anniversary programme. The best questions — the ones that show genuine preparation and go somewhere the public record hasn't — may actually be asked.

What makes a question worth asking?

It is not answerable from existing interviews, papers, or public statements. If the answer is already in the Turing Award transcript, the question isn't ready.

It is specific enough to require a specific answer. "What did it feel like to change the world?" is not a question. "You've described the moment Merkle's puzzle idea reached you — but I couldn't find anywhere that you described what you were working on the day before, and whether that work influenced how you understood his idea" is a question.

It asks about a person's experience, not a concept. The concepts are documented. The experience of the person living through the moment is not.

It acknowledges that minds change. A question that references what someone said in 1991 and asks how they think about it now treats the source as a thinking person, not a fixed document.

A note on changing minds
The interviews in Lab 3 span more than three decades. Hellman's 1991 account of the NSA's opposition is not the same as his 2004 account. Merkle's framing of his contribution has shifted as the historical record around it has become clearer. When you ask your local sources about events from twenty or thirty years ago, you are capturing how they understand those events now — which is itself part of the history. Both what they remember and how they remember it are data.
Module B2 — The interview itself

You have done the preparation. Now you are sitting with someone who was there. Here is how to make the most of it.

1
Open with context, not flattery
Tell them briefly who you are, what you are investigating, and why their perspective is specifically relevant. Not "you are so important to this history" but "I'm investigating how digital signatures first reached [country], and I understand you were involved in [specific thing]. I've been reading [specific sources] and I'm trying to understand [specific gap]." This tells them you have done the work — and earns a more substantive conversation.
2
Ask for the story, then ask about the story
Let them tell the account in their own sequence first — even if you know parts of it already. Then go back with specifics: "You mentioned [X] — can you tell me more about who else was in the room for that decision?" Following up on what they just said is almost always more productive than moving to your next prepared question.
3
Sit with silence
When someone stops speaking, wait. Don't fill the silence with the next question. People often say the most important thing in the pause after they think they've finished. Silence is uncomfortable — resist the urge to end it prematurely.
4
Ask about people, not concepts
"Who did you call first?" produces more than "how did you feel?" Ask about specific people, specific days, specific conversations. "Walk me through the day you realised X" opens more than "tell me about X." Oral history lives in the concrete.
5
Close with the open door
Always end with: "Is there something about this story that you've never been asked, that you think matters?" or "Is there someone else I should talk to who would give me a perspective I haven't heard?" The first question often produces the most important answer. The second builds your source network.
6
Record with permission, note without it
Always ask before recording. If they decline, take detailed notes immediately after the conversation — not during, if note-taking makes them uncomfortable. Write up everything you remember within an hour of the interview, while it is still fresh. Memory degrades faster than you expect.
Reflection — before your first interview
Prepare your opening and your close for your local source

Write out exactly how you will open the conversation with the first person you plan to interview in your own investigation. Then write your closing question. Practice both until they feel natural — not scripted.

  • Your opening: two sentences that tell them who you are, what you're investigating, and why you specifically wanted to speak with them
  • Your first question: specific, shows preparation, invites them to tell the story in their own sequence
  • Your closing: the open-door question that invites what you haven't thought to ask
Module B2 — When the story doesn't add up

Two sources, same event, different accounts. This is not a problem. It is the material.

History is not a fixed record waiting to be retrieved. It is a collection of partial accounts, each shaped by the position, memory, and interests of the person giving it. When your sources contradict each other — and they will — you are not seeing an error. You are seeing how history actually works.

"The disagreement between two honest accounts is not noise. It is signal about the event itself — about who saw what, from where, and what they chose to remember."
How to work with contradiction
1
Document both accounts precisely
Before you try to resolve anything, record exactly what each source says. Quote directly where possible. Don't paraphrase yet — paraphrase is interpretation, and interpretation comes later. Get the raw accounts first.
2
Identify what kind of contradiction it is
Some contradictions are factual — two people disagree on a date, a name, a sequence of events. Others are interpretive — they agree on what happened but not on what it meant. Others are positional — each person can only have seen part of what happened from where they stood. Each type calls for different handling.
3
Look for corroborating evidence
Can a document, a date, or a third account settle a factual dispute? If so, use it — and note that you did. If the documentary record agrees with one account, say so. If it doesn't resolve the dispute, say that too. The process of looking for corroboration is itself part of your methodology.
4
Present contradiction honestly
You do not have to choose a winner. When two credible sources genuinely disagree and you cannot resolve it, your job is to present both accounts, explain why they might differ, and let the contradiction stand. "Source A says X. Source B says Y. The documentary record does not resolve this" is a legitimate historical finding.
5
Ask what the contradiction reveals
Why might two people remember this differently? What does each account protect or promote? Whose interests does each version serve? These are not cynical questions — they are analytical ones. The disagreement often reveals something about the event itself that neither account captures alone.
Activity — practice with the pioneer record
Find a contradiction in the Diffie-Hellman-Merkle interviews and work through it

Using the interviews from Lab 3, find a moment where two accounts of the same event don't fully align. Apply the five steps above to that contradiction.

  • Document both accounts precisely — what exactly does each person say?
  • What type of contradiction is it — factual, interpretive, or positional?
  • Is there a document or third account that corroborates one version over the other?
  • What does the contradiction reveal about how each person understood their role in the story?

Write this up in your log. You are practicing the analytical move that will be essential when your own sources contradict each other.

Module B2 — The ethics of the work

You are asking people to share things they may never have said publicly before. That comes with obligations.

1
Obtain informed consent
Before any interview, explain clearly: who you are, what the interview is for, where it will be stored, who will have access to it, and whether it will be published or attributed. Ask explicitly whether they consent to being recorded, quoted, and named. Get this in writing if possible — at minimum, record verbal consent at the start of a recorded interview.
2
Honour agreed boundaries
If someone says something is off the record, it is off the record — permanently, not just for now. If they ask to review their quotes before publication, agree to that, and follow through. The relationship of trust with your source is more important than any single piece of information.
3
Protect sources who didn't ask to be protected
Sometimes a source shares something that could put them at risk — professionally, politically, personally — without realising it. You are responsible for thinking about that risk even when they haven't. If publishing or sharing an account could cause harm to the person who gave it, think carefully before you do. When in doubt, ask them.
4
Give credit accurately
The history of cryptography is partly a story of misattributed and uncredited work. Don't replicate that in your own record. When something came from a specific person, name them. When you are uncertain of the source, say so. Precision about attribution is not just academic practice — it is respect for the people whose work you are documenting.
5
Remember whose story this is
You are a historian, not a journalist looking for a story. Your job is to preserve and transmit — accurately, fairly, with the complexity intact. The people you interview are not sources to be used. They are participants in a history that belongs to them as much as to anyone.
If your investigation touches sensitive territory
Some local histories of cryptographic control and access are politically sensitive. If your investigation raises safety concerns — for you or for your sources — connect with your regional coordinator before proceeding. There are resources specifically designed for this situation, separate from this handbook.
