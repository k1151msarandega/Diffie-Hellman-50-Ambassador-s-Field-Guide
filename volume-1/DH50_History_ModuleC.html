Module C — The analytical moment

You have been in the field. You have found things, missed things, been surprised, and hit walls. Now you sit with what you have — and this is where the real work begins.

Raw material is not a finding. An interview transcript is not history. A document is not an argument. The move from material to meaning requires a specific analytical discipline — and it starts with an honest inventory of exactly what you have, sorted by how well you actually know it.

"I don't know yet" is not a failure state. It is the most honest thing an investigator can say — and the engine that keeps the work moving forward."

Outstanding History Ambassadors do not inflate what they have. They do not present speculation as finding, inference as evidence, or a single source as confirmation. They are precise about what they know, what they suspect, and what they cannot yet say. That precision is what makes their contribution credible — and lasting.

Take inventory before you do anything else

Before you try to write up a finding, before you share anything with other ambassadors, sit down and categorise everything you have gathered. Not by topic — by how well you know it.

Confirmed — multiple independent sources agree
Two or more sources that don't share an origin point tell the same story. A document corroborates what a person said. This is your solid ground. You can assert these things directly.
Supported — one good source, consistent with other evidence
A single credible primary source says something, and nothing you've found contradicts it. You can present this, but with attribution: "According to [source]..." rather than stating it as established fact.
Contested — sources disagree, or evidence is ambiguous
Two sources tell different stories, or a single source makes a claim that other evidence complicates. Present both sides. Do not resolve it artificially. "Source A says X. Source B says Y. The documentary record does not settle this" is a legitimate finding.
Absent — you looked and didn't find it
You searched for something and it isn't in the record. This is not nothing — it is a finding about the record itself. Document what you looked for, where you looked, and what you found instead. Absence, rigorously documented, is evidence.

The inventory is not a defeat
Many ambassadors arrive at this point expecting more confirmed findings than they have. The honest inventory often shows more "supported" and "absent" than expected. That is not a sign of poor fieldwork — it is a sign of rigorous thinking. A small, well-evidenced finding is worth more than a large, poorly-supported one. And a documented absence is a genuine contribution to the global record.
Module C — The analytical move

A finding is not what you found. It is what the evidence shows — stated as precisely as the evidence allows, no more and no less.

This distinction matters enormously. "I found a 1994 document showing the Central Bank of [country] issued a directive about digital certificates" is what you found. "The Central Bank's 1994 directive was the first official recognition in [country] that cryptographic authentication had legal standing — predating the commercial deployment of SSL by a year" is a finding. It takes what you found and places it in a context that gives it meaning.

The analytical move has three parts: what the evidence shows, what it means in context, and what you are not claiming.

How to build a finding
1
State what the evidence actually shows
Start with the most concrete thing your strongest evidence demonstrates. Not what you believe, not what would make sense — what the evidence directly supports. Use language that matches your confidence level: "confirms," "suggests," "indicates," "is consistent with." These are not the same word.
2
Situate it — what does this mean beyond itself?
A finding isolated from context is a fact. A finding in context is history. Why does this matter? What does it reveal about how cryptographic trust arrived in your region — the timing, the actors, the decisions, the exclusions? How does it fit with or complicate what the global record already shows?
3
State explicitly what you are not claiming
This is the step most investigators skip — and it is the most important one for credibility. "This finding does not establish X" or "I cannot determine from available evidence whether Y" is not weakness. It is precision. It tells the reader exactly where your finding ends and where further investigation is needed.
4
Name the gaps that remain
Every finding leaves questions open. Name them. "To fully understand this, someone would need to find X" or "the missing piece is Y, which I could not locate because Z." This is not an apology — it is a gift to the next person who investigates this story. Your documented gaps become their starting points.
5
Test it against a sceptical reader
Before you share any finding, ask: what would a careful, sceptical reader push back on? Where is the argument weakest? What alternative interpretation does your evidence allow? If you can answer those questions, your finding is ready. If you can't, it needs more work — or more careful framing.
Activity C1 — Write your finding
Draft a finding statement from your strongest evidence

Using the five steps above, write a finding statement from the strongest confirmed or well-supported item in your inventory. Keep it to a paragraph — precision matters more than length.

Your finding statement should answer four questions in sequence:

  • What does my strongest evidence directly show? (stated with appropriate confidence language)
  • What does this mean in the context of how cryptographic trust arrived in my region?
  • What am I explicitly not claiming from this evidence?
  • What gap does this finding reveal that someone else should investigate?

Write this in the finding record in the next tab. That record is the version that travels — into the global repository, to other ambassadors, into the permanent archive of this initiative.

On writing about uncertainty without undermining your finding

There is a difference between honest uncertainty and hedging that renders a finding meaningless. "It seems like possibly something might have happened" is not a finding. "The evidence strongly suggests X, though I could not independently verify Y" is.

Calibrated language — matching the strength of your claim to the strength of your evidence — is a skill. Practice it here, in this module, before your finding enters the permanent record. The goal is a finding that is as strong as your evidence allows, and no stronger.

Module C — Connecting your thread to the whole

Your finding is a piece of a worldwide picture. But a piece only contributes to the picture if it is connected to it explicitly.

The global story of how cryptographic trust traveled from a Stanford lab in 1976 to the digital infrastructure of daily life is being assembled from thousands of local stories — each one partial, each one necessary. Yours is one of them. The work of this tab is to make that connection visible: to show how what you found in your community adds something to the global record that wasn't there before.

Three questions that connect the local to the global
1
What does your finding add that wasn't in the record?
The global record of DHM's impact is heavily weighted toward the US, Europe, and a handful of well-documented Asian deployments. If your finding comes from anywhere else — or from an undocumented aspect of a known deployment — it is adding something genuinely new. State what that is explicitly. Don't assume the reader will see it without help.
2
Which thread from Module A does your finding connect to?
Go back to the four discovery quests. Does your local story echo the Merkle pattern — independent work that arrived through the wrong channel? The GCHQ pattern — institutional secrecy that shaped what became public? The RSA pattern — a cascade of deployment following a foundational breakthrough? The export control pattern — access shaped by political decisions? Naming the connection makes your finding legible to the global picture.
3
What does the global story look like differently because of what you found?
This is the hardest question — and the most important one. Does your finding confirm the global pattern, or complicate it? Did cryptographic trust arrive in your region through a route the standard account doesn't describe? Did it arrive later, earlier, or more unevenly than the global narrative suggests? A finding that complicates the standard story is more valuable than one that confirms it.
Activity C2 — Make the connection explicit
Write the paragraph that places your local finding in the global story

This is a single paragraph — three to five sentences — that does the following:

  • States your finding in one sentence
  • Names which global thread it connects to and how
  • Says what the global story looks like differently because of what you found — confirmed, complicated, or extended
  • Names what question your finding opens for other ambassadors investigating similar stories elsewhere

This paragraph goes into the global repository as the "connector" statement for your finding. It is how ambassadors in other countries will understand what your local investigation contributes to the shared picture.

Your absence is also part of the global picture
If the most significant thing you found is that the record doesn't exist — that there is no documentation of how cryptographic trust arrived in your region, that the people who built it are unreachable or have died, that the institutional memory has been lost — that is itself a contribution. It tells the global picture where the gaps are. Document it as carefully as you would document a confirmed finding.

Module C — Learning from each other

You are not the only person doing this work. Across the world, ambassadors are investigating the same idea as it landed in different places, at different times, under different circumstances.

The Global Connector exchange is where that network becomes active. You share what you found. You receive what someone investigating a different region found. You note what their finding reveals about yours — and what yours might reveal about theirs. Knowledge multiplied is the phrase used in the DH50 framework. This is what it looks like in practice.

Global Connector Exchange
What you share — and what you receive

When you submit your finding record, you will be connected with one or two ambassadors investigating different regions whose work touches a similar thread. The exchange works in both directions: you receive their finding record, they receive yours. You each respond to the other's work with the questions below.

This is not peer review — it is cross-pollination. You are not judging each other's work. You are asking what each other's findings open up when seen from a different geography.

When you receive another ambassador's finding
Four questions to ask of their work
  • Does their finding confirm, complicate, or extend something you found in your own investigation? How?
  • Is there a person, institution, or document in their finding that has a parallel in your region — someone you hadn't thought to look for?
  • What does their experience of doing this investigation — their note to other ambassadors — change about how you understand your own process?
  • What is the one question their finding opens that you would want them to pursue next?
Global connector log
The exchange is ongoing — not a one-time event
The Global Connector exchange is not a single submission and response. As your investigation develops, your finding record updates — and so does what you can offer other ambassadors. Return to this tab whenever a significant new finding changes the picture. The network gets smarter as each of you goes deeper.
