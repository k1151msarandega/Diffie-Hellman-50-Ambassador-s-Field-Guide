Module 0.0 — Why you're here

This is not an archive. It is a rescue mission.

In 1976, an idea emerged from a Stanford lab that changed how every human being on earth communicates. You almost certainly used it this morning. The people who built the first systems on top of it — who deployed it in banks, government offices, and trading networks around the world — are aging. Their stories are not written down. In many cases, they exist nowhere except in memory.

Your job as a History Ambassador is to find those stories before they are lost — starting in your own community, your own region, your own corner of the world.

"How did this idea, born in a Stanford lab in 1976, travel to here — and change this community's relationship with trust, commerce, and identity?"

That is the question at the heart of your work. Not a general history of cryptography — there are books for that. The specific, unrepeatable story of how cryptographic trust arrived where you are. Or didn't arrive. Or arrived unevenly, for some people and not others.

This field guide will walk alongside you through that investigation. You don't need to read it front to back. Pick it up when you need a framework, a method, or a prompt to get unstuck. The work is yours. The guide just helps you do it.

Before you go further
Three questions worth sitting with

You don't need to answer these now. But keep them close — they will sharpen as you work.

  • When did people in your community first start trusting digital systems with something that mattered — money, identity, health records? What made that possible?
  • Who in your region was involved in building or deploying that trust? Are they findable?
  • What parts of this story might disappear if no one records them in the next few years?
What an outstanding History Ambassador does
They don't just search online. They go to people — the engineers, administrators, and policymakers who were actually there. They ask questions nobody has thought to ask. They find primary sources in places secondary sources don't mention. And they bring back something the world didn't have before.

This handbook was created as part of the Diffie-Hellman 50th Anniversary Initiative, a global project to document how public-key cryptography traveled from a 1976 paper to the digital infrastructure we depend on today.

Module 0.1 — The briefing

The idea that changed everything

In 1976, Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman published a paper in the IEEE Transactions on Information Theory. It was eleven pages long. Its title was modest: New Directions in Cryptography. What it contained was not modest at all.

For centuries, secure communication had required a shared secret — a key, a codebook, a prior meeting between the people who needed to communicate. This was fine for diplomats with couriers and armies with codebooks. It was a fundamental barrier for everyone else. You could not have a private conversation with a stranger over an open channel — not without having already met them somewhere private first.

"They showed that two parties who had never met — who shared no prior secret — could nonetheless establish a shared secret that no eavesdropper could crack."

Diffie and Hellman dissolved that barrier. The locked box could be sent without the key. The key could be constructed, independently, at both ends. This was not an improvement on existing cryptography. It was a different kind of thing entirely.

Within a year, three mathematicians at MIT — Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Len Adleman — used Diffie and Hellman's framework to build something further: a system for digital signatures. Not just private communication, but verifiable identity. A way to prove, mathematically, that a message came from who it claimed to come from, and had not been altered in transit.

These two ideas — public key exchange and digital signatures — are the foundation beneath nearly everything you do online today. The padlock in your browser. The payment you made this morning. The message that arrived with your name on it.

Discovery quest — go deeper
The story has fringes worth following

The 1976 paper is where this story starts — but not where the interesting parts live. As you work through this handbook, you'll find quests that trace the stranger, less-told threads:

  • Ralph Merkle's puzzle concept — developed independently, it informed Diffie and Hellman's work in ways the history books often skip over
  • GCHQ's parallel secret — British intelligence solved the same problem years earlier and classified it; the world didn't know until 1997
  • The export control battles — the US government treated strong cryptography as a weapon and tried to control its spread
  • The first deployments — who actually built the first real systems, and where, and under what circumstances?

Each thread has a local echo. The export control story is a story about which countries got access and when. The deployment story played out differently in every region. Your investigation starts here and reaches outward.

Module 0.2 — The mathematics, made tangible

You don't need to understand the mathematics to do this work. But spending five minutes with it will make the magic real in a way that reading about it cannot.

The core problem Diffie and Hellman solved: how do two people agree on a shared secret over a channel that anyone can listen to, without ever sending the secret itself? Here is exactly how it works — in four steps.

1
Public setup
Both parties agree on two public numbers that anyone can know: a large prime number p, and a base generator g. In the demo below, p = 23 and g = 5. In real systems, p has hundreds of digits.
2
Private keys
Alice secretly picks a private number a. Bob secretly picks a private number b. Neither tells the other. These never leave their hands.
3
Public exchange
Alice computes A = ga mod p and sends A to Bob — openly, over the public channel. Bob computes B = gb mod p and sends B to Alice. An eavesdropper can see both A and B. That's fine.
4
Shared secret
Alice takes Bob's public value and computes s = Ba mod p. Bob takes Alice's public value and computes s = Ab mod p. Because of the commutative property of modular arithmetic, both calculations produce the same number — the shared secret. Neither sent it. The eavesdropper, who only has A and B, cannot work backwards to find it without solving a problem that is computationally infeasible for large numbers.

The eavesdropper sees g, p, A, and B — all four public values. To find the shared secret, they would need to reverse the modular exponentiation: given A = ga mod p, find a. This is the discrete logarithm problem. For the small numbers here, it's solvable by trial and error. For numbers with hundreds of digits, no computer built or conceivable could do it in any practical timeframe. That gap between easy and impossible is what makes the whole system work.
Reflection
What just happened?

Try a few different combinations of private numbers. Notice that the shared secret always matches — and that the public values alone don't reveal it.

  • What would an eavesdropper need to know to crack this? What makes that hard with large numbers?
  • Why did this idea take until 1976? What had to exist — mathematically, computationally — before it was possible?
  • When you explain this to someone in your community who has never thought about it — what analogy will you use?
Module 0.3 — Your ambassador log

Every investigation needs a record. Not a report — a thinking tool. Your log is where you note what surprised you, what you're not sure about, who you need to find, and what you're starting to understand that you didn't before.

You'll return to it after every significant piece of work. By the end of the year, it will be a record of how your thinking developed — and that record matters. Not just for you, but for the ambassadors who come after you, investigating the same questions in different places.
